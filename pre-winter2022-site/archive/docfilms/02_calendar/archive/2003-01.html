Sidney Poitier and the Civil Rights Era

January 12       7:00
Cry, the Beloved Country
Zoltan Korda, 1951     103m

Alan Paton wrote this rarely-screened adaptation of his novel of the same name, which stars a dashing young Sidney Poitier as Reverend Msimangu. Msimangu is a missionary-schooled, black South African priest, whose asexuality, gradualist politics, and cool-headed sophistication became part of Sidney Poitier's trademark "unreality." Poitier's appearance in this once-controversial film also demonstrates the actor's larger concerns with mining literary material for African-American performance and addressing social problems through films.       


January 19       7:00
Intruder in the Dust
Clarence Brown, 1949              87m

Intruder is adapted from William Faulkner's novel about politics in the South. Ralph Ellison wrote of Intruder: "Seriously, [it] is the only  film that could be shown in Harlem without arousing unintended laughter."                35mm


January 26       7:00
Blackboard Jungle
Richard Brooks, 1955            101m

Resonant with angst and machismo of troubled youth as well as tremors of racial tension, Blackboard Jungle gave Poitier his coolest role as a physically fit and mentally robust youth with no regard for authority. Released one year after the Supreme Court overturned public school segregation, the film's producers expected to strike a nerve, tagging it as "A shock story of today's high school hoodlums!"

The Defiant Ones

Lilies of the Field
Ralph Nelson, 1963   94m

Poitier's Oscar-winning performance as a reluctant handyman among busy nuns in the Southwest takes a low-key pace and warm sentimental charm, giving the feel of a peaceable American parable. While applauded for his performance, Poitier took some criticism for what many saw as a black-accommodationist performance during the often violent confrontations of the Civil Rights movement. Lilies of the Field will be preceded by newsreel footage of Dr. Martin Luther King's  "I Have A Dream" address. 35mm

Contemporary French

January 6         7:00
Happenstance
Laurent Firode, 2001 90m

Can seemingly random events and insignificant actions have far-reaching consequences?  On the way to work, Irène (Amélie's Audrey Tautou) meets a woman on the metro who predicts that she will meet the love of her life that very day.  Irène exchanges sly glances with the young man across the aisle, but the two are soon separated. Through a staggering array of missed opportunities, unexpected turns, and chance meetings, they end up together. Happenstance explores the operations of fate not only with philosophical insight, but also with humor and charm.                      


January 13       7:00
Time Out
Laurent Cantet, 2001 132m

Vincent, fired from his job, doesn't really want to find another one. Unable to tell his family, he leaves for work to drive around aimlessly. Coming across a management center associated with the United Nations, he decides this would make a fine pretend career. While he is initially able to maintain his comfortable position by borrowing money from his parents, Vincent must resort to swindling his friends with a fraudulent investment scheme, and an importer of fake designer-label merchandise approaches him with a real (illegal) employment offer. Time Out explores the essential insubstantiality of modern-day corporate management.           


January 20       7:00
Lumumba
Raoul Peck, 2000      115m

This engrossing and insightful biography traces the rise to power, brief tenure, and execution of Patrice Lumumba, the first Prime Minister of independent Congo.  A charismatic, committed, and uncompromising leader, Lumumba espoused a vision of a united Congo in which the creation of a strong national government would overcome inter-tribal conflict. Without glorifying its protagonist, Lumumba presents a thought-provoking examination of the difficulties faced by African politicians who attempt to create cohesive, functional states despite the legacy of colonialism, continued interference from imperial powers, and the pressures of the Cold War.     


January 27       7:00
The Piano Teacher
Michael Haneke, 2001         130m

Erika Kohut (Isabelle Huppert) is a respected piano instructor and scholar at the Vienna Conservatory.  When Walter, a handsome, self-confident member of her master class, falls in love with her, she consents to his advances but demands control of their relationship. Erika orders him to perform sadistic sexual practices on her, and Walter's desire turns to repulsion. Walter's reaction and departure precipitates Erika's emotional disintegration. Hanke juxtaposes the formal and elegant spaces of the conservatory with the coarse sex shops and lavatories that each reflect part of Erika.  Piano Teacher won the Best Actress, Best Actor, and Grand Jury prizes at Cannes.


February 3       7:00
Murderous Maids
Jean-Pierre Denis, 2000      94m

Based on the true story of the Papin sisters, who brutally murdered and disfigured their employers in 1933, Murderous Maids is a deftly-crafted look at the life of domestic servitude of two girls in early 20th century France. Christine Papin, (Sylvie Testud) one of three daughters, dreams of becoming a nun, but is condemned by her mother (Isabelle Renauld) to "slave for others" as she has done for her entire life. Christine arranges for her youngest sister, Lea (Julie-Marie Parmentier of I'm Going Home), to join her as a maid at the Lancelin household, where they cling to each other as their only comfort. Their co-dependency evolves into an intimate sexual relationship, which is a surprise and a delight to both sisters.
The story of the Papin sister has been taken up by many writers over the past seventy years, including Jean Genet ("The Maids") and most by recently Nancy Meckler, in her 1994 film, Sister My Sister. At times, the Papins' story has been portrayed as one of class struggle, or as proof of the violent and demented roots of homosexuality. Director Jean-Pierre Denis treats the story more carefully, focusing on the very personal psychological development of the two girls. Testud gives a chilling and emotional performance as Christine Papin, portraying her gradual descent into madness (which psychologists would later diagnose as paranoid schizophrenia). Christine is dominating and jealous, but also terrified. She alternates between fitful outbursts and trembling in fear. Lea, by comparison, is portrayed as innocent and naïve, falling helplessly into the great storm of her sister's madness, and participating in her employers' murders out of devotion to Christine.


February 10    7:00
Va savoir
Jacques Rivette, 2001            154m

Rivette's nimble romantic comedy charts the amorous entanglements of six characters through a changing arrangement of couples. Camille (Jeanne Balibar), an actor in an Italian theatre troupe who left her lover Pierre (Jacques Bonnaffé) three years earlier, returns to Paris for a short run of a Pirandello play in which she plays the lead. Ugo (Sergio Castellitto), the play's director and Camille's current lover, obsesses about finding the text of a lost 18th century Italian play rumored to be in Paris. Full of witty dialogue and winning performances, the film's light touch and, for Rivette (La Belle Noiseuse), moderate length, make it one of the director's most accessible.


February 17     7:00
Read My Lips
Jacques Audiard, 2001          115m

Carla Bhem (Emmanuelle Devos), a secretary for a property development company, reads lips due to a hearing impairment. Work frustrates Carla, but her young, inexperienced trainee, Paul (Vincent Cassel), proves quite interesting. Carla hires Paul after his release from prison for reasons more personal than professional. She teaches him good manners and he teaches her naughty ones, and through his criminal connections the two become involved in a heist. However, their characters, not the crime, provide the lynchpin of this film. This unlikely couple, both unsatisfied in their respective roles, share a workplace and a joint criminal venture, and more.      


February 24     7:00
8 Women
François Ozon, 2002             103m

Playing out like a demented Clue, 8 Women is a quirky French murder mystery with a musical twist. It is another joyous Christmas in the French countryside…until Marcel, the male host, is found stabbed and dead.  In the midst of a heavy snowstorm, the only culprits are the eight equally suspicious women trapped in the house. Among them are an ungrieving wife, two loving daughters,  Marcel's sister, an aging virgin, two lying maids, and an alcoholic granny.  Starring a literal who's-who of French actresses (Catherine Deneuve and Emmanuelle Beart, to name just a few), 8 Women is an estrogen-laden event that both men and women are sure to enjoy.


March 3          7:00
How I Killed My Father
Anne Fontaine, 2002 98m

Not the step-by-step confessional the title suggests, How I Killed My Father belongs to a class of deeply psychological French dramas. Anne Fontaine, the director of the droll Dry Cleaning, takes on the Freudian conflict between father and son. Forty year-old Jean-Luc (Charles Berling) is a successful gerontologist living in the wealthy Parisian suburb of Versailles with his beautiful wife, Isa (Natacha Regnier).Jean-Luc appears to have everything one could want from life, but when his long estranged father Maurice (Michel Bouquet) unexpected arrives, it threatens to shatter Jean-Luc's facade. Michel Bouquet won the 2002 César for Best Actor.         


March 10        7:00
In Praise of Love
Jean-Luc Godard, 2001         97m

Edgar (Bruno Putzulu) is preparing a project on the theme of love, which will follow couples of three ages through love's four stages: meeting, passion, separation and reconciliation. But Edgar doesn't yet know what form the final project will take. While auditioning actors, he encounters a woman (Cécile Camp) perfect for a role, but when he decides to cast her he learns that she has died. The first part of the film is shot in a luminous black-and-white reminiscent of Godard's work of the 1960's, and even revisits some of the same locations. In the second part, set two years earlier, Godard uses super-saturated video to convey the sense of a still vibrant past.   


Fast-Talking Dames

January 7         7:00
Dinner at Eight
George Cukor, 1933   113m

Jean Harlow heads an all-star cast in this luscious Art Deco adaptation of the famous George S. Kaufman and Edna Ferber play. Dinner at Eight concerns an ambitious Park Avenue socialite and her attempts to arrange a dinner party for an aristocratic couple and their guests, played by Wallace Beery, Lionel Barrymore, John Barrymore and the incomparable comedienne Marie Dressler. Playing a delightfully vulgar nouveau riche hussy who turns out to be whole lot shrewder than she initially seems, Harlow scores a comic triumph in this standout performance. She delivers her lines with great gusto, wringing out every last drop of comic juice.      


January 14       7:00
The Thin Man
W.S. Van Dyke, 1934            93m

After an early career in which she was typecast as an "exotic" (read: Asian) temptress, Myrna Loy finally came into her own playing the smart, sassy, sexy "perfect wife," Nora Charles. In this film, Nick (William Powell) and Nora are at their irreverent, cocktail-swilling best as they investigate the mysterious disappearance of an eccentric inventor. The Thin Man, based on the novel by Dashiell Hammett, took only 16 days to film and was a hugely popular success. Audiences responded to the playful banter between Powell and Loy, which set a new standard for wit and sophistication in the movies. Come see this groundbreaking portrayal of a marriage between equals.

Easy Living
Mitchell Leisen, 1937            88m

It's the height of the Depression, and a millionaire (Edward Arnold) throws a mink coat out the window, which lands on Mary Smith's (Jean Arthur) head. As if this weren't enough luck for this plucky working girl, a series of surprise incidences catapult her into the upper crust of New York society. This lovely, little romantic comedy gem boasts a screenplay by Preston Sturges (Sullivan's Travels, The Lady Eve), and the terrific supporting cast includes many performers who were to become Sturges regulars, such as William Demarest and Franklin Pangborn.

Twentieth Century
Howard Hawks, 1934 91m

This screening will be introduced by the author of Fast-Talking Dames, Professor Maria DiBattista.
If you give a woman an inch, she'll take a mile. If you give Lily Garland (aka Mildred Plotka, played by the divalicious Carole Lomard) stardom, she'll take it and run. When Broadway bigshot Oscar Jaffe (John Barrymore), makes a starlet of little Miss Plotka, he finds that the former lingerie model won't stand by her man through his theatrical flops, bankruptcy, and faked suicides. February 4      7:00
Mr. Deeds Goes to Town
Frank Capra, 1936      115m

This delightful comedy stars Gary Cooper as the sappy, but painfully sincere greeting card poet, Longfellow Deeds. When Mr. Deeds unexpectedly inherits a fortune and visits New York, his own small-town values are tested in the face of big-city corruption and cynicism. Husky-voiced Jean Arthur co-stars as Babe Bennett, the reporter whose hardened exterior hides a tender heart. Mr. Deeds' "share the wealth" redistribution plan is a prime example of the New Deal populism that infused so many of Capra's Depression-era films. But instead of getting bogged down in its own seriousness; Capra's sure-handed touch and shrewd folk humor keep this film aloft.


February 11     7:00
Stage Door
Gregory La Cava, 1937           92m

This memorable and supremely entertaining film concerns the triumphs and heartaches of life in the theater. Set in a boarding house for young actresses, the film's atmosphere is pervaded by a joyous spirit of wisecracking female comaraderie. The stellar ensemble cast is led by Katherine Hepburn, as a wealthy and somewhat snooty newcomer, and Ginger Rogers as her earthy, working class roommate. They are supported by some of the wittiest character actresses of the era, including Eve Arden, Lucille Ball, and Ann Miller, allowing much of the film to be improvised on the set. La Cava sustains a tone that's a tricky blend of pathos and smart-mouthed comedy.


February 18     7:00
The Awful Truth
Leo McCarey, 1937    91m

This giddy, stylish, and largely improvised screwball comedy concerns a couple (Cary Grant and Irene Dunne) who are about to divorce in order to marry other partners. But first, they have to settle their custody battle over Mr. Smith, their dog (played by Asta, the talented terrier who also starred in Bringing Up Baby and the Thin Man series). This sparklingly funny movie features a top-notch supporting cast, including Ralph Bellamy in his oft-played role of the dull fiancé who loses the girl to Cary Grant. Director Leo McCarey, who paired Stan Laurel with Oliver Hardy and directed the Marx Brothers, lends his unquestionable eye for comedy to this film.     


February 25     7:00
Bringing Up Baby
Howard Hawks, 1938 102m

This masterpiece of lunatic comedy features a dizzy heiress (Katherine Hepburn), a nerdy paleontologist (Cary Grant), a dog named George, and a pet leopard named Baby. At the movie's outset, Grant's character is simultaneously trying to manage the arrival of a rare dinosaur bone, a meeting with a wealthy donor, and his marriage to the ultra-proper Miss Swallow (Virginia Walker). But the arrival of  Hepburn, the madcap heroine who brings chaos wherever she goes, turns his life upside down. Directed by Howard Hawks, this fast-paced, absurdist farce is centered, thematically, on the principles of wildness and anarchy vs. order and propriety.

David Lynch

January 8         7:00
Pretty as a Picture
Toby Keeler, 1997     85m

Most closely associated with David Lynch's film Lost Highway, Pretty as a Picture looks at Lynch's career in motion pictures, his bizarre paintings, and his intriguing life. Lynch grew up in small town America, or rather several small American towns, as his father constantly moved the family. The unmistakable Lynchian small town (Lumberton, Twin Peaks) certainly arose from this period, while his first feature, Eraserhead, owes more to his time in Philadelphia. Before that film, later a cult classic, Lynch experimented with several short films ("Six Figures Getting Sick," "The Amputee")  included in part here.


January 15       7:00
Eraserhead
David Lynch, 1977     90m

David Lynch shot this, his debut feature film as a film student in Philadelphia. The story centers around Henry Spencer (Jack Nance), a young man struggling with the idea of fatherhood. His wife, Mary X, gives birth to a monstrous, deformed baby whose wail threatens to push her over the edge. Befriended by a woman who lives in his radiator, Spencer finally decides to murder his own child. Set in a nightmarish industrial landscape, this five-year project has all the surreal, horrific trimmings of Lynch's later, higher-budget films. Lynch described this masterpiece as "a dream of dark and troubling things". Freudians, take delight!


January 22       7:00
The Elephant Man
David Lynch, 1980    124m

The Elephant Man tells the story of John Merrick (John Hurt), a deformed man with Proteus Syndrome who is part of a circus freak show in Victorian England. A surgeon, Sir Frederick Treves (Anthony Hopkins), rescues Merrick from the cruel circus world and gives him housing, but when Merrick meets a stage performer (Anne Bancroft), his growing fame forces him again to face those who ridicule him. Based on the chronicle by Treves, this film was nominated for eight Academy Awards, including Best Actor, Best Picture, and Best Director. The moral tale is carried by strong, compassionate performances by John Hurt and Anthony Hopkins.            


January 29       7:00
Dune
David Lynch, 1984     137m

This unique epic, adapted from Frank Herbert's novel, surpasses the visual interest and displays more imagination  than the standard science fiction fare. The application of Lynch's style to the science fiction medium results in some of the most striking and cohesive sci-fi set design since Blade Runner. The futuristic story of Paul Atreides (Kyle McLachlan) and the strategically important, parched, worm-ridden planet Dune—the only location of a certain life-enhancing spice—features a cast of thousands. It is Lynch's most ambitious film to date. Look for the very young Sting as Feyd-Rautha and for Patrick Stewart as the ever-faithful Gurney Halleck.


February 5       7:00
Blue Velvet
David Lynch, 1986     120m

When they say "you can never go home again," they don't usually mean a severed human ear is waiting for you. Jeffrey Beaumont (played by Lynch regular Kyle MacLachlan) makes the old mistake, and finds the usual sleepy streets, blue skies, red roses, white picket fences, and that severed human ear. Perhaps the most beautiful of all of his films, Lynch's skill as a painter is apparent in the careful composition of an indelible scene featuring Isabella Rossellini (one of history's most beautiful women) lying naked on a manicured lawn while simultaneously conveying brutality, dignity and desire. Blindly sensual, the film is a low-budget Halloween party.              


February 12       7:00
Wild at Heart
David Lynch, 1990    124m

Jiu-jitsu kicking Sailor Ripley (Nicholas Cage) is released from prison into the arms of his Lula (Laura Dern). The two hit the road for the Deep South, pursued by a killer (the extra creepy Willem Dafoe) hired by Lula's mom. A highway tale both weird and wild throughout, this Palme D'Or winner faced censorship in the United States for, among other things, the severity of Sailor's temper. Featuring Chris Isaak's "Wicked Game" and Lynch's first use of Sherilyn Fenn (whom he would later cast as Audrey Horne in "Twin Peaks"), Wild at Heart is a primary text for the American highway-romance genre, rivaling even Bonnie and Clyde.      


February 19     7:00
Twin Peaks: Fire Walk with Me
David Lynch, 1992     135m

David Bowie, Kyle MacLachlan, and Heather Graham star in this prequel to Lynch's TV series of the same name.  Perhaps the most bizarre of any Pacific Northwest town, Twin Peaks (before Laura Palmer's death) hosts FBI agent Desmond (Chris Isaak) as he investigates Teresa Banks' murder.  He runs across all sorts of weirdos  and everything to be expected of a filmmaker who makes a film inaccessible to all except those who have followed the TV show and already understand the plot. Or if not "plot," at least viewers who have acquired a certain "Twin Peaks" vocabulary.


February 26     7:00
Lost Highway
David Lynch, 1997     135m

David Lynch is a director who makes movies that are often impossible to understand and, sometimes, even to like. And he doesn't seem to care, as long as he likes them. Lost Highway creates what Lynch calls a "dramatic fugue" where the dualism of the character becomes literal when a jazz musician (Bill Pullman), in jail for killing his wife (Patricia Arquette), literally becomes another person. (Balthazar Getty). Lost Highway exudes an eerie atmosphere; an almost palpable presence all the more chilling because of its enveloping dreamlike irrationality.  Expect the mystery man, another version of Arquette, and plenty of typical Lynch oddities.


March 5          7:00
The Straight Story
David Lynch, 1999                    111m

The premise might sound a little hokey (Alvin Straight, a blind, old man, drives hundreds of miles across the Midwest on his lawnmower to visit his estranged brother), but in David Lynch's hands The Straight Story blossoms into a genuine and moving tale. Richard Farnsworth captures the stubborn septuagenarian with a wonderfully stoic performance and Sissy Spacek is outstanding as Straight's daughter, but the real stars of the film are Lynch's surprisingly understated direction and cinematographer Freddie Francis' spectacular shots of Iowa in late fall. When Straight finally reaches his destination, the effect is magisterial and magical.


March 12        7:00
Mulholland Drive
David Lynch, 2001    147m

Lynch called Mulholland Drive "A love story in the City of Dreams." Of course, despite the cheerful sunshine and pretty people, Lynch's City of Dreams rests upon a bedrock of weird, dark, and incomprehensible (sometimes criminal) currents. Into this world of deceptive façades comes Betty (Naomi Watts), a ray of cheerful sunshine from the great happy province of Ontario. Her outlook on life in Los Angeles harkens back to the bumpkin disposition of cheap novels and Shirley Temple movies: But in the this Nathanael West world, nothing is possible. Into her (aunt's) sunny apartment falls an amnesiac Rita and a complicated mystery developes.

Aimée and Jaguar
Max Färberböck, 1999          125m

This film is about two women during WWII. Felice organizes for the Jewish underground and works by day as the assistant of a Nazi newspaper editor. Lilly is married to a Nazi officer, has four children, and is awarded the Nazi "mother cross". Felice seduces Lilly as a joke; and they fall in love. Even when presented with the chance to leave Germany, Felice stays to be with her lover until August 1944 when the police begin to catch on. Based on fact, this narrative feature won critical praise and was a festival/art-house hit.

This free screening will be followed by a conversation with  Director Sandi Simcha Dubowski.

March 6                          7:00
The Man Who Drove with Mandela
