Mondays - Cool Films from Cold Countries: Recent Nordic Film

September 27 7:00
Open Hearts
Susanne Bier, 2002 113m

When her fiancé is paralyzed from a car accident, Cecilie (Sonja Richter) finds more than just comfort in the arms of a married hospital doctor. The dogme 95 film takes a turn towards the melodramatic when the doctor's wife is revealed to be the other person in the car accident. Although "tragic accident" and "adulterous affairs" may seem like the stuff of soap operas, Bier's film transcends those conventions by focussing on the responsibility the lovers feel for those they are leaving behind. A very popular filmmaker in her native country of Denmark, this is Bier's first work to reach the states. 35mm

October 4 7:00
Elling
Petter Næss, 2001 89m

Elling is the story of a middle-aged man who is forced to finally grow up. After his mom/roommate dies, Elling can't cope and is sent to a state facility. His roommate turns out to be Kjell, a 40-year-old virgin obsessed with sex. Two years later, the friends give the real world a try again, with hilarious results. Their adventures in writing, sex, and car ownership are both funny and dark. The screenwriter, Axel Hellstenius, created the film out of a series of Norwegian short stories. One of the highest grossing Norwegian films of all time, Elling was nominated for a foreign language Oscar. 35mm

October 11 7:00
Nói albonói
Dagur Kári, 2003 93m

Like many people who live in vast but sparsely populated areas, Nói is lonely. And, like many Icelanders, his isolation doesn't prevent him from being a hipster. This tale of Nói's senior year of high school—the challenges of living with a grandmother who uses a shotgun as an alarm clock, falling in love in the shadow of ice mountains—is incredibly moving. It also features a beautiful trip through a taxidermy museum. Nói albonói swept international award festivals, not only picking up statues for its acting and cinematography, but also for its original sound track. Director Kári is also in the band slowbow, which scored the film. Those Icelanders!

October 18 7:00
101 Reykjavik
Baltasar Kormákur, 2000 88m

This "Icelandic slacker film" is probably the most widely viewed Icelandic film ever. The slacker is 28-year-old Hulfner, who lives with his mom on welfare. Things heat up when his mother and girlfriend start having an affair and everyone is forced to reconsider what makes a family. This film is more than just a black comedy, it's a fun look into the more nontraditional dynamics of Icelandic relationships. Director Kormákur, who was previously a popular Icelandic actor, took the title from the address of his bar (and home) in Reykjavik.

October 25 7:00
Together
Lukas Moodysson, 2000 106m

Together chronicles the funny, yet sad and boring moments of living in a Swedish commune in the mid-1970s. Moodysson (Lilya 4-ever) indulges in lots of period details (yes, there is ABBA music), but focuses his attention on the characters, particularly those of Elisabeth and her children, who move to the commune to escape her alcoholic husband. This film escapes easy idealizing or bashing of the commune experience, instead approaching it thoughtfully. Like many Swedish films, Together is both very serious and very funny.

November 1 7:00
Songs from the Second Floor
Roy Andersson, 2000 98 m

After spending 25 years making "the best TV commercials in the world" (so said Ingmar Bergman) Andersson created this particularly non-commercial film. A series of loosely connected scenes are set in a European city, dulled by an unknown economic crisis. Inside this almost static drabness, Andersson creates scenes of extreme originality and humor using a cast of nonprofessional, but excellent, actors (Lars Nordh, one of the main actors, was discovered by Anderson at an IKEA store). The film takes a typically critical approach to materialism, but in the most atypical of ways. 35mm

November 8 7:00
The Man Without a Past
Aki Kaurismäki, 2002 97m

Kaurismäki is probably the most popular Finnish filmmaker and this film is one of his very best. It tells the story of a traveling salesman who is beaten up by a couple of thugs while on a business trip. He wakes up in a poor, remote town, unable to remember who he is, yet still able to seduce a lonely Salvation Army worker. Typical of the director's films, this one features an amazing sound track of Finnish rock and lounge music. It also features "Tahti", the famous dog actress. The best part about Kaurismäki's films are his quirky characters living on the edge of society, something much admired by his biggest fan, Jim Jarmusch. 35mm

November 15 7:00
Mifune
Soren Kragh-Jacobsen, 1999 99m

The third film to come out of Denmark's Dogme school, Mifune tells the story of Kresten, a rising businessman in Copenhagen, who has just married his boss's daughter. He hasn't told her—or anyone—that he came from a poor farming family. On their honeymoon, Kresten finds out that his father died, and he returns home to look after the family farm and care for his mentally disabled brother. He places an ad for a housekeeper, which is answered by a prostitute named Liva, who is trying to escape a psychotic client and make money for her brother's school tuition. A heartwarming and fresh study on the conflicting pulls of success and family roots.

November 22 7:00
Italian for Beginners
Lone Scherfig 118m

For those who thought that Dogme filmaking was all about Bjork and hankerchiefs, this film is bound to be a surprise. An actual Dogme romantic comedy, Italian for Beginners follows the lives of several students taking an intro Italian class in a suburb of Copenhagen. The film mostly follows their interactions in the classroom complex, but also on a trip to Venice, where the characters are revealed in a new light. Director and writer Scherfig (Wilbur Wants to Kill Himself) mantains the intimacy that Dogme filmaking allows—to the point that the viewer almost feels involved in the action—but adds a welcome touch of lightness and humor. 35mm

November 29 7:00
Insomnia
Erik Skjoldbjærg, 1997 97m

At first Insomnia may seem like the typical murder mystery movie, but it quickly becomes something much more. In an excellent performance, Stellan Skarsgård (Breaking the Waves and Good Will Hunting) plays Jonas Engström, an Oslo cop sent to a small town in Northern Norway to solve the mystery of a teenage girls' death. But things go wrong from the beginning and Engström finds himself struggling not only to find the killer, but to save himself. Skjoldbjærg uses the perpetual daylight and cold atmosphere of Norway to great effect. Even if you have seen the 2001 remake, this original version of the film will surprise and shock you. (Plus, this one's better.)


Tuesdays - Occupation, Colonialism, Human Rights

A discussion will follow each film.

Co-sponsored by the Human Rights Program, Center for the Study of Race, Politics, and Culture, Committee on Japanese Studies, Action Towards Peace, and Student Government Finance Committee.

September 28 7:00
The Sorrow and the Pity
Marcel Ophüls, 1971 260m

Arguably the best documentary ever made, this film is probably best known for two reasons: it was Woody Allen's date movie in Annie Hall and it is important to French constructions of history. Originally banned from TV by the French government, it challenged the then-prevalent myth in France of heroic resistance to the Nazi occupation. It is a collage in which Marcel Ophüls, son of director Max Ophüls, intersperses contemporary newsreel footage and propaganda films into dozens of interviews with French, British, and German citizens, soldiers, former Nazis, members of the resistance, and collaborators.

October 5 7:00
The Battle of Algiers
Gillo Pontecorvo, 1965 120m

Pontecorvo's seminal docu-drama depicting the events leading to Algerian independence in 1962 is a work of stunning realism, heavily employing amateur actors while imitating a newsreel style. The narrative focuses on the massive rebellion launched by the Algerian National Liberation Front (FLN) between 1954 and 1957, showing the organization of the resistance movement and the tactics used by the French to destroy it. Pontecorvo was effectively on the side of the Algerian rebels, yet he eschews glamorization of either side in the conflict. New Print! 35mm

October 12 7:00
Franz Fanon: Black Skin, White Mask
Isaac Julien, 1996 70m

Julien explores the life and work of one of the 20th century's pre-eminent theorists on race, colonialism, and identity. Born in Martinique and educated in France, Fanon was a psychiatrist in Algeria who eventually joined the FLN. Transformed by these experiences, he authored revolutionary theories on the psychological relationship between the colonized and colonizer. Julien integrates readings of Fanon's work, reenactments of key moments in his life, and interviews with friends, family, and cultural theorists, illuminating the implications of his ideas in the contemporary world.

October 19 7:00
Arna's Children
Juliano Mer-Khamis & D. Danniel, 2003 84 m

Arna's Children follows a children's theater group, established by the Isræli Arna Mer-Khamis, in the West Bank town of Jenin. Drawing from footage recorded during rehearsal periods from 1989 to 1996, Juliano focuses on the lives of a few of the children. He then returns to see what has happened to them following the notorious battle in Jenin in 2002. This provocative documentary is an intense look at both terrorism and the Isræli occupation.

Like Twenty Impossibles
Annemarie Jacir, 2003 17m

Palestinian filmmakers try to enter Jerusalem after a chekpoint closes, revealing the indignity of life in occupied territories.

October 26 7:00
Untamagiru
Takamine Go, 1989 120m

A post-colonial rendition of an Okinawan folk-myth, this film is set in 1969, three years before Okinawa was returned to Japan after 27 years of American occupation. Giru is a sugar factory worker in love with the pig-incarnate daughter of his boss, Nishihara. When Nishihara threatens to kill him, Giru flees to the forest, where a spirit endows him with superhuman powers. Using his newfound capacities, he steals from the rich to give to the poor, sabotaging both American and Japanese economic interests. Deftly employing color, song, and dance, this hilarious film explores the culture and identity of Okinawa and its relationship with its historic occupiers.

November 2 7:00
Emitai
Ousmane Sembene, 1971 103m

Senegalese director Ousmane Sembene, often considered the father of African cinema, is known for his emphasis on historical interpretation and his criticism of local corruption. Based on a confrontation that occurred during World War II between the inhabitants of a Diola village in Senegal and the French military, this film reveals the tragic role of military collaboration. After the colonial government conscripts the young men in the village as soldiers, it demands a tax payment in rice to feed the army. Led by the women and elders, the villagers refuse, and a conflict ensues. The final scene was blacked out by the French government, and the film was banned in Senegal after its release. 35mm

November 9 7:00
Gandhi
Richard Attenborough, 1982 187m

Beginning with Mahatma Gandhi's assassination in 1948 at the hands of Nathuram Godse, and then rewinding to 1890s South Africa, the film stars Ben Kingsley (in his most memorable role) as Gandhi, a Westernized lawyer with reformist goals fighting for the civil rights of Indians as citizens within the British Empire. Attenborough succeeds in conveying Gandhi's charismatic authority, his political intellect, and the role of his peculiar mix of Western liberalism and Hindu philosophy in the form of his philosophy of nonviolence. This film won 8 of the 11 Academy Awards that it was nominated for, including best actor and best film. 35mm

November 16 7:00
Burn!
Gillo Pontecorvo, 1970 112 m

Burn! is Pontecorvo's sequel to Battle of Algiers, and serves as an allegory for the then-ongoing U.S. intervention in Vietnam. Sir William Walker (Marlon Brando) is a British agent hired to provoke a slave revolt against the Portuguese in the fictional colony of Queimada in the Antilles in the mid-19th century. Seeking to make room for British economic interests, he incites Jose Dolores, a water porter, to lead the insurrection. Ten years later he returns, hired by a British authority, the Antilles Royal Sugar Company, to quell a revolt of sugar plantation workers led by Dolores. This often-overlooked performance from the late Brando is superb. 16mm

November 23 7:00
Kanehsatake: 270 Years of Resistance
Alanis Obomsawin, 1993 120m

In 1990, the municipality of Oka, Quebec approved a plan to develop a luxury housing complex and golf course that would appropriate land from the nearby Mohawk reservation. In response, the Mohawks staged a protest that developed into a historic confrontation with Quebec's police and the Canadian Army, initiating a self-determination movement among indigenous groups throughout Canada. Obomsawin recorded the extraordinary footage from the Mohawk side of the barricades over a grueling 78-day standoff. She also documents the contemporary political and social structure of the reservation inhabitants. 35mm

November 30 7:00
Rabbit-Proof Fence
Phillip Noyce, 2002 93m

Until 1970, the Australian government forcibly removed "half-castes" (children of both Aboriginal and white descent) from their homes, in hopes that their Aboriginal roots could be gradually "stamped out" via integration into white society. This film is based on the exceptional story of three such children of the so-called "Stolen Generations." Placed in a dormitory in 1931 to be trained as servants, the girls escape and attempt to find their way back home by following a rabbit-proof fence that runs across the continent. 35mm


Wednesdays - Mizoguchi

Cosponsored by the Japan Committee of the Center for East Asian Studies, the Department of East Asian Languages and Civilizations, and the Committee on Cinema and Media Studies.

September 29 7:00
Hometown
Kenji Mizoguchi, 1930 86m

Mizoguchi's first talkie, this melodrama concerns an ambitious singer (played by famous opera tenor Yoshie Fujiwara) and the conflict between artistic integrity and his desire for fame and fortune. When his first record becomes a hit, success goes to his head, and he begins to ignore his loyal girlfriend in favor of his patron, a rich society woman. This fascinating early work deals with such characteristic Mizoguchi themes as the perils of ambition and the ubiquity of female-sacrifice. It also contains striking examples of Mizoguchi's experimentation with Western cinematic techniques such as tracking, dolly shots, montage sequences, and close-ups. Archival print. 35mm

October 6 7:00
The Story of the Last Chrysanthemums
Kenji Mizoguchi, 1939 142m

In 19th century Tokyo, a young Kabuki actor from a prominent theatrical family discovers that he is praised because of his connections, not his talent. Determined to succeed on his own merits, he leaves home, accompanied only by a loving young female servant who believes in his art. Constructed with the fluid camerawork and graceful long shots that were to become the director's hallmark, this exquisite shinpa tragedy about female self-sacrifice and the pursuit of artistic excellence is perhaps the greatest of Mizoguchi's prewar efforts. 35mm

October 13 7:00
The 47 Ronin, Pts. I and II
Kenji Mizoguchi, 1941 219m

Based on Japan's most popular samurai legend, this film, which was not seen in the West until 1979, was made at the government's behest and conceived as nationalist propaganda. A lord is dishonored by his enemies. His men, reduced to the lowly status of ronin (masterless samurai), then seek vengeance for their master's humiliation. Mizoguchi downplayed the story's action and violence in favor of contemplation and emotion. Of the many film versions of the story, this one, with its striking visuals, careful attention to character, and moving images of loyalty and self-sacrifice, is generally considered the greatest. Archival print. 35mm

October 20 7:00
My Love Has Been Burning
Kenji Mizoguchi, 1949 84m

This film, part of Mizoguchi's "fighting women" trilogy, is probably his most explicitly feminist work. Loosely based on the autobiography of Eiko Kageyama (Kinuyo Tanaka), a late 19th century crusader for women's rights, the film follows Kageyama as she leaves her job as a schoolteacher, becomes involved in the new Liberal Party in Tokyo, and struggles to reconcile her feminism with her relationships with men. Distinguished by its remarkably modern sexual politics, the film is unsparing in its exposure of the hypocritical private lives of the "progressive" male politicians. It ends with a hauntingly powerful image of solidarity between two women. 35mm

October 27 7:00
Life of Oharu
Kenji Mizoguchi, 1952 148m

Mizoguchi considered this to be his finest work, and it was his first film to gain international renown. Based on a 17th century novel, it tells the story of Oharu (Kinuyo Tanaka), a lady of the imperial court who, following an illicit affair with a lower class page, is exiled and sold into concubinage, eventually resorting to begging and street prostitution. This work is a harrowing chronicle of the oppression of women, but Mizoguchi balances scathing social criticism with great formal beauty and a serene visual style. 35mm

November 3 7:00
Sisters of the Gion
Kenji Mizoguchi, 1936 69 m

This film, set in Kyoto's Gion district, concerns a geisha who initiates her younger sister into the trade; the relationship between the two dramatizes the conflict between tradition and modernity. The older woman values loyalty to her patrons, while the younger one believes they should put their own interests first. But the sisters learn that whether they follow tradition or reject it, their lowly status as geishas and as women ensures that they share the same, tragic fate. Often considered the best Japanese film of the prewar era, it was the only Mizoguchi film to win Japan's top film prize, the Kinema Junpo award. 16mm

November 10 7:00
Ugetsu
Kenji Mizoguchi, 1953 96 m

This film will be introduced by Mizoguchi scholar Chika Kinoshita. In war-torn 16th-century Japan, two peasants leave home seeking wealth and accomplishment. But in achieving his goals, each man brings tragedy to himself and his family. Based on a Japanese fairy tale and incorporating elements from the Noh theatrical tradition, this deeply poetic film illustrates the Zen principle of mono no ware, an acceptance of the inherent sadness of life and the transience of all things. (Mizoguchi converted to Buddhism shortly after making this film). Ugetsu is frequently cited as one of the masterpieces of world cinema. 35mm

November 17 7:00
Sansho the Bailiff
Kenji Mizoguchi, 1954 125 m

In this haunting tale of tyranny, sacrifice, and salvation set in 11th century Japan, a provincial governor is sent into exile, his wife is forced into prostitution, and his son and daughter are sold as slaves. Raised by the brutal tax collector Sansho, the son grows into a cruel and bitter man, until a memory from his past forces him to question what he has become. This magisterial, lovingly detailed film movingly illustrates the maxim, "without mercy, a man is not a man." The beautiful photography is by the great cinematographer Kazuo Miyagawa, who makes extensive use of Mizoguchi's characteristic long takes and deep-focus shots. 35mm

November 24 7:00
Osaka Elegy
Kenji Mizoguchi, 1936 71m

A landmark in the development of realism in Japanese cinema, this powerful social protest film concerns a young telephone operator who becomes her boss' mistress in order to pay her father's debts. Gradually, she slips into a life of prostitution, and though she eventually becomes the sole support of her entire family, they reject her for what she has become. Osaka Elegy marked Mizoguchi's first collaboration with screenwriter Yoshikata Yoda, and, though he had been making films for 15 years, it was not until this film that he came into his own as director. He said, "I was able finally to learn to show life as I see it." 16mm

December 1 7:00
Street of Shame
Kenji Mizoguchi, 1956 88m

In this, his final film, Mizoguchi abandoned his earlier lyricism for a harsher naturalism. At Dreamland, an ironically named Tokyo brothel, five women have turned to prostitution for economic survival in devastated postwar Japan. As a child, Mizoguchi saw his own sister sold to a geisha house, and in his films he demonstrated profound sympathy for geishas and prostitutes. It has been argued that this film—a portrait of their relationships, dreams, and frustrations— was influential in outlawing prostitution in Japan, which was banned there shortly after the film's release. 35mm


Thursdays 1 - Mockumentary

September 30 7:00
Culloden/The Wargame
Peter Watkins, 1969/1965 72/42m

These two films represent the beginnings of the Mockumentary genre as well as amazing claims to truth. Culloden is a re-enactment of the legendary Scottish war between the Highland forces of Bonnie Prince Charles and the British army; much of this is, by necessity, fictional. The second film is a truer Mockumentary, and portrays the horror of a postnuclear war England. Though it was meant to air on the virtually censor-less BBC, it was considered too intense and violent to broadcast. Although fictional, the film actually won an Oscar for best Documentary. 16mm

October 7 7:00
Fear of a Black Hat
Rusty Cundieff, 1994 88m

What Spinal Tap did to Heavy Metal, Fear of a Black Hat does to the world of rap. NWH (Niggaz With Hats, as opposed to the real-life NWA, Niggaz With Attitude) must win over their fans, while still maintaining their image as angry young black men. The band's name comes from the invented idea that, in the days of slavery, field hands were forced to work in the sun without hats so that they would be too tired to revolt. Complete with parodies of every hit single of the era, this is a must for anyone interested in music. 35mm

October 14 7:00
Zelig
Woody Allen, 1983 79m

Comic genius Woody Allen brings a touch of class to the genre by getting talking heads like Susan Sontag and Saul Bellow to corroborate his story. Leonard Zelig (Allen), the quirky protagonist, is able to become the people who surround him. Among the obese, he gains tremendous amounts of weight; in Chinatown he begins speaking perfect Mandarin. Set in the roaring '20s, Allen uses innovative editing techniques to place himself alongside major luminaries of the time like Babe Ruth and Woodrow Wilson. 35mm

October 21 7:00
Waiting for Guffman
Christopher Guest, 1996 84m

Christopher Guest (This is Spinal Tap) is often credited with the popularization of the Mockumentary genre. In this off-beat comedy about the 150th anniversary of a small Missouri town, an off-off-off-Broadway director (writer/director Guest) produces a musical featuring a cast of the town's locals. The great ensemble cast is best at acting badly, from the dentist who yearns for stardom (Eugene Levy), to the DQ-employee (Parker Posey), to the All-American couple (Catherine O'Hara and Fred Willard). 35mm

October 28 7:00
Bob Roberts
Tim Robbins, 1992 102m

If George W.'s down-home credibility came from being a folk-singer, he might look a lot like Bob Roberts (Robbins), a millionaire who runs for the US Senate. Making use of the entertainment culture of American politics, Roberts hides his corrupt campaign goals (gunsfor-drugs trades) behind a compassionate conservative veneer. Robbins blurs the line between real and fake through cameos by actors (Helen Hunt and Susan Sarandon) and experts (Gore Vidal) to create a film that's both funny and frightening. 35mm

November 4 7:00
Man Bites Dog
Rémy Belveaux, 1992 95m

Ben is a vicious serial killer which, in today's television world, makes him the perfect protagonist for a new series. A camera crew is hired to follow him around as he kills, steals, and waxes intellectually about the arts, literature, and life. In the end, he is so engaging that the crew itself is sucked into his morally mishmashed world. Though often graphically disturbing, the film's subtle humor and charm win over the real-life viewers as well; this film won the International Critic's Prize at Cannes.

November 11 7:00
Nothing So Strange
Brian Flemming, 2002 82m

This bizarre and rarely-seen film begins with the assassination of Bill Gates on December 2, 1999. As the requisite mourning period comes to a close, an independent group called Citizens for Truth launches an aggressive investigation into the killing, leading them into conflicts with the gratuitously mainstream press and the quasi-villainous LAPD. Using footage that imitates the Kennedy assassination and the genre of conspiracy theory that surrounded it, Flemming succeeds in a revolutionary blurring of the fictional world and the real one. DVD

November 18 7:00
Close-Up
Abbas Kiarostami, 1990 100m

Renowned Iranian director Kiarostami documents the trial of an unemployed film fanatic who has been arrested for impersonating the famous director Mohsen Makmalbaf and telling a stranger that he wants to make a documentary about her family. This improbable story is true, but, just as in the real-life case, it is unclear who are actors or actual participants. The tricked family remains at the center of a famous director's film, just not as they had envisioned. An ambiguous mix of dramatization and original footage.

December 2 7:00
The Beaver Trilogy
Trent Harris, 2001 83m

"Groovin" Gary is a young man from Beaver, Utah with a remarkable talent: dressed in a massive blonde wig and black leather, he does off-key renditions of Olivia Newton John's oeuvre. The first film is simply Trent Harris's 1979 documenting of Gary's story and his struggle with his conservative town. In the second film, made in 1981, a young Sean Penn does his own Groovin' Gary impersonation with some added narrative. The final film, from 1985, turns Gary (this time played by Crispin Glover) from a pathetic fool into a tragic hero. DVD


Thursday 2 - Jim Jarmusch and Hal Hartley

September 30 9:45
Stranger than Paradise
Jim Jarmusch, 1983 89m

Jarmusch's second feature establishes his ambivalence to traditional modes of storytelling, which he later explained by saying: "Life has no plot, why must films or fiction?" A low-budget, rambling affair, this introduces us to a mood-driven filmmaking that's thin on story but delivers humor, character depth, and emotion with a gentle and grainy touch. John Lurie plays a drifter, Willie, who heads with his buddy Eddie on a road trip in order to find Willie's Hungarian cousin, Eva. b/w 35mm

October 7 9:15
Down by Law
Jim Jarmusch, 1986 107m

Described by the filmmaker as a "neobeat-noir-comedy," it features Lurie again, now with famed hobo balladeer Tom Waits and Italian comic Roberto Benigni. Lurie and Waits are too laid back to avoid being framed for crimes, and the three meet up in and escape from a New Orleans jail. Their escape leads them through dense swamplands and perhaps denser humor. Robby Müller's cinematography is gorgeous; the lush shots of Louisiana's wilderness look forward to Dead Man. b/w

October 14 9:00
Mystery Train
Jim Jarmusch, 1989 113m

This film begins with a Japanese couple that travels to Memphis; she likes Elvis, he likes Carl Perkins. Two more storylines emerge from chance encounters at the hotel the couple is staying at. As Jarmusch continues his comic view of America through the eyes of outsiders, he extends his experiments with narrative, inviting us to make connections between characters, stories, and locations. The nice thing is that the connections are neither forced nor profound. Beautiful cinematography by Müller, but this time in color. 35mm

October 21 9:00
Night on Earth
Jim Jarmusch, 1991 129m

Five cabbies in five cities, and their conversations with passengers. We see a number of the Jarmusch regulars return, from actors (Roberto Benigni and Tom Waits) to stylistic preferences (multiple stories, emphasis on character studies). The stories occur in roughly sequential moments of time, including Winona Ryder and Gena Rowlands discussing—what else—movies in L.A. Jarmusch pushes narrative logic to the extreme by means of a loosely sequential temporal flow. 16mm

October 28 9:30
Dead Man
Jim Jarmusch, 1995 121m

A brilliant revision of the Western, this film is not to be missed on the big screen. With a humming, coughing, and soaring musical score by Neil Young, this is Jarmusch at his best. Johnny Depp plays William Blake, an accountant from Ohio who travels out West and becomes a killer and, perhaps, a visionary poet. Witty and strange, like other Jarmusch films, this one has a stronger sense of purpose behind its loose narrative, emerging as a commentary on the American wilderness and our place within it. b/w 35mm

November 4 9:15
Amateur
Hal Hartley, 1994 104m

By the time Hartley's fifth feature came out, he had already forged a cult following for his offbeat art films. Loaded with black humor, metaphysical ponderings, and bizarre storylines, his films also had distinctive visual style, with overdetermined colors and stylized compositions. Amateur is more accessible than his earlier work but no less strange. A man wakes up bloodied with no memory; an ex-nun and aspiring pornographer helps him as he tries to piece together his identity. 16mm

November 11 9:00
The Book of Life
Hal Hartley, 1998 63m

This comedy about the second coming of Christ was made during the shooting of Henry Fool. Jesus shows up in New York on the last day of the century to perform the Last Judgment and initiate the end of the world, but he's having second thoughts. This film is part of the French "Collection 2000 Seen By" television series of films about the millenium. With P. J. Harvey as Jesus's assistant, Magdalena, and Thomas Jay Ryan as Satan.

November 18 9:30
Henry Fool
Hal Hartley, 1997 137m

This film marks a shift in Hartley's style, as he jettisons stark colors, flat walls, and aloof characters for warm, textured images and flesh and bone characters. In this loose fable about art and society, Henry is a rebellious drifter, an intellectual ego-maniac, and an ex-convict boozer who moves into Simon's basement. Simon's a garbage man who already has his depressed mother and over-sexed sister to deal with. While Henry labors on his masterwork, Simon manages to publish a work that brings him both fame and scandal.

December 2 9:00
No Such Thing
Hal Hartley, 2002 105m

A foul-mouthed Monster, disgusted with human evolution and the vagaries of consumer society, has taken to killing anyone who crosses his path (which happens to be in Iceland). He is impervious to nuclear radiation, he's indestructible, and it's unclear that he'll ever be gotten rid of. A journalist for a sensationalistic TV news program forges a relationship with him that develops as the film builds its fable about the negative effects of a shallow media. An updated "Beauty and the Beast," but through Hartley's idiosyncratic and stylish lens. 35mm


Weekend - Harold & Kumar Go To Doc

October 1 6:30, 9:00, 11:30
Super Size Me
Morgan Spurlock, 2004 96m

Setting out to prove the evils of the American fast food industry, Morgan Spurlock personally assumes the serious health risks of a lifestyle that is all-too quickly becoming the American way: little exercise and lots of French fries. The rules are simple: for 30 days Spurlock can consume only food and drink offered on the McDonalds menu, he has to consume three squares a day, and he must supersize his meal if offered. Spurlock makes a convincing case for passing on a big Mac. His unnerving physical and emotional breakdown is accompanied by a fascinating examination of politics, consumerism, and education in the fast food industry. 35mm

October 2 6:30, 9:00, 11:30

Way Down East
D. W. Griffith, 1920 149m

This film, adapted from a Victorian stage melodrama, has a Tess of the D'Urbervilles-type plot: an innocent waif from the country (Lillian Gish) is seduced and abandoned by a cad from the city. The story may be familiar, but Griffith infuses it with such emotional directness and storytelling flair that it transcends its genre. The result is one of Griffith's greatest works, second only to The Birth of a Nation in popularity. Gish's incandescent performance, the vivid on-location photography, and a spectacular finale on the ice floes help make this film an emotionally overwhelmingly experience. 16mm

Camille
George Cukor, 1936 109m

Based on a Dumas novel (also the source for Verdi's La Traviata), this is the classic hooker-with-a-heart-of-gold story, focusing on the doomed romance between a consumptive, self-sacrificing prostitute (Greta Garbo, in one of her finest performances) and a handsome young nobleman (Robert Taylor). It is frequently cited as one of Hollywood's greatest romantic movies. Garbo was never more beautiful, and her warm and delicately ironic performance is quietly heartbreaking.
