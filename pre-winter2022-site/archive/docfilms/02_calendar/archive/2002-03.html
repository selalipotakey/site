Summer 2002 Series Calendar

 
June 26 8:00
The Circus
Charles Chaplin, 1928 71m

Charlie Chaplin's Tramp, one of cinema's most beloved and hapless characters, vies for the love of the daughter of the ringmaster of a circus. But the Tramp is chased by just about everybody from the police to the ringmaster himself.

Two Tars
James Parrott, 1928 30m

Two sailors (Stan Laurel and Oliver Hardy) take their dates out for a ride, but become involved in a traffic mishap.

 
June 27 8:00
Verboten!
Sam Fuller, 1959 93m

Immediately after the Second World War, American soldiers stationed in Berlin were not allowed fraternization with the civilians: such relations were Verboten. But Sgt. Brent cannot resist the romantic pull of the German woman who saved his life. The situation becomes more complicated when her younger brother, a devoted Nazi, seeks to use Brent for his own dastardly means. From Beethoven mixed with sniper fire in the opening sequence, Sam Fuller knows no subtlety, causing Verboten! to powerfully and unabashedly shout from every frame.

 
June 28 7:00, 9:15
The Bicycle Thief
Vittorio DeSica, 1949 90m

This neorealist classic depicts the plight of Antonio, an unemployed worker in post-WWII Italy. When he finally finds a job putting up posters, his overjoyed family pawns their bed linens in order to buy him the bicycle necessary for his new job. On his first day of work, the bicycle is stolen, and Antonio and his young son Bruno embark on a search through the streets of Rome - with a tragic conclusion. Touchingly portrayed by amateur actors, the characters invite us to reflect on human dignity at its most desperate. 35mm

 
June 29 7:00, 9:15
Delicatessen
M. Caro & J. Jeunet, 1992 95m

Writers and directors Marc Caro and Jean-Pierre Jeunet (City of Lost Children) serve up a blacker-than-black comedy set in a surrealistic post-apocalyptic hell. In a desperate society in which food is so rare it's currency, an ex-clown seeks employment in the delicatessen of an eccentric landlord/butcher of questionable morals. When our young hero falls in love with the butcher's daughter, he comes up against his employer's ulterior motives. A delectably quirky look at greed and, yes, commodification, done with striking imagination. 35mm

 
July 3 8:00
The Brothers Karamazov
E. Engles & F. Otsep, 1931 93m

This version of Dostoyevsky's classic novel was written for the screen, directed by the German Engles and the Russian Otsep and filmed in Germany two years before the appointment of Hitler to the Chancellorship. While the story itself is common, this version stands as one of the most innovative in its use of photography, editing, and sound in the early sound period. The musical score by composer Karol Rathaus has received much praise.

 
July 4 8:00
Mr. Smith Goes To Washington
Frank Capra, 1939 130m

Jimmy Stewart plays Jefferson Smith, who fills the seat of a recently deceased senator. Corrupted politicians are surprised when he uncovers rampant corruption in the Senate and retaliate by creating false scandal around Smith. The film culminates with Smith's 23-hour filibuster in which Stewart gives an outstanding performance as an American man who is weary but undeterred in his stand for justice. A stirring tribute to the power of democratic process. God Bless America! 35mm

 
July 5 7:00, 9:30
Rififi
Jules Dassin, 1955 115m Jules Dassin's masterpiece (for which he won Best director at Cannes) follows Tony le Stephanois as he is released from jail and joins a group of thieves planning to knock over a jewelry store. At the center of the film is the tense and detailed thirty-minute heist sequence, executed completely without dialogue, that set the standard for all future heist films. The plan goes off perfectly, but as the criminals sit on their 240 million francs payoff, things start to go wrong. 35mm

 
July 6 7:00, 9:30
Lock, Stock and Two Smoking Barrels
Guy Ritchie, 1999 105m

This roller-coaster of a film is the predecessor to director Guy Ritchie's Snatch. In Lock, Stock and Two Smoking Barrels, four friends find themselves owing a fortune when they gamble on a rigged card game. They come up with a hasty scheme in order to save their fingers from amputation, but soon all the local crooks find themselves intertwined in a plot where only karma will triumph. Fast editing make this British comedy in the tradition of Tarantino, only kitschier. 35mm

 
July 10 8:00
Loulou
Maurice Pialat, 1980 110m

A wealthy businesswoman (Isabelle Huppert) leaves her life of comfort and safety to shack up with a working class brute (Gerard Depardieu) in this cruel, brash film of fits and starts.  Full of fear and exhilaration, the two lovers collaborate with a gang of thieves. The film focuses on the most ordinary of effects: an empty street after an evening at the bar and the tautness of sheets on a newly made bed.  There is no heavy-handed psychology, no morality, and no inevitability; only drunken stumbling down blind alleys toward broken bedframes. 35mm

 
July 11 8:00
I Am Cuba
Mikhail Kalatozov, 1964 141m

Once Khrushchev's and Castros' glorification of the Cuban revolution and a poetic and cinematic piece, I Am Cuba defies comparison. Four stories present the situation in Cuba before the revolution. Two poets collaborated on the screenplay, and the film maintains a lyrical feeling throughout. Riots and pool parties, the agrarian hills and the city are all the subject of the elaborate, fluid camera movements, and are all accompanied by quickly-paced Cuban music. 35mm

 
July 12 7:00, 9:45
Chinatown
Roman Polanski, 1974 131m

Broken lenses, flawed irises, blurry photographs, mistaken identities: "You may think you know what you're dealin' with, but believe me, you don't," warns water baron Noah Cross in Roman Polanski's revisionary take on the classic whodunit detective story. Robert Towne's Oscar-winning screenplay, with its intricate twists and witty dialogue, honors the motifs of film noir while simultaneously shattering them. Starring Jack Nicholson, Faye Dunaway and John Huston. 35mm

 
July 13 7:00, 9:15
The Night of the Hunter
Charles Laughton, 1955 91m

Robert Mitchum is Harry Powell, a murderer posing as a preacher, in this critically acclaimed, although idiosyncratic, suspense thriller. When his father is imprisoned, nine-year-old John Harper (Billy Chapin) must watch over his younger sister Pearl (Sally Jane Bruce) as well as the loot stolen by his father. Unfortunately for John, Harry comes to town in search of the stolen money. The film is now renowned for the very oddities that hindered its commercial success. 35mm

 
July 17 8:00
Mississippi Masala
Mira Nair, 1992 118m

Mira Nair (Monsoon Wedding) directed this tale about an Indian family who moves to Mississippi to run a roadside motel after being expelled from Uganda. Once in America, their daughter Mina falls in love with Demetrius (Denzel Washington), a young black man. Mina's parents are horrified by the relationship and forbid Mina from seeing Demetrius. Eventually, Mina's parents must compare their racism with the racism that forced them from their homeland. 35mm

 
July 18 8:00
WWII Shorts
WWII Era approximately 140m

During the Second World War, the United States government issued informational short films to program with longer features. These shorts concerned the progress of troops at the front, the contribution of science to the war, and the need to keep working for victory. Hollywood crews and famed directors produced the shorts anonymously on studio lots in between work on longer features. In this collection, see Ronald Reagan accidentally shoot at an American plane, learn how to act when initially exposed to poison gas, and more!

 
July 19 7:00, 9:30
Tampopo
Juzo Itami, 1986 114m

When Goro cannot find a satisfactory bowl of noodles he sets himself upon a valiant mission: to create the perfect noodle shop. He takes Tampopo under his wing and trains her to the most exacting noodle-creation standards. The film follows Goro's pursuit of perfection. With satires of cooks, cowboys (Itami referred to his movie as a “Noodle Western”), Hollywood romances and Japanese society, Tampopo is a fresh comedy that manages to make even noodles racy. 35mm

 
July 20 7:00, 9:15
Swingers
Doug Liman, 1996 96m

Mike (Jon Favreau) leaves New York City for Los Angeles, leaving someone very special behind. His friend Trent (Vince Vaughn) does everything he can to push Mike back into the dating circuit. Mike's friends are there for him in a childish yet mature pursuit of love and happiness reminiscent of real life. Swingers has everything from the revival of swing music to the ridiculous rules of dating. The journey ends when Mike meets Lorraine (Heather Graham). 35mm

 
July 24 8:00
Mexican Bus Ride
Luis Bunuel, 1952 74m

Bunuel's only documentary, this is the plight of the poor people of Las Hurdes in Spain, where fresh bread is unknown, and the only break from routine is cruel death.

Land Without Bread
Luis Bunuel, 1933 27m

Oliverio Grajales' mother is dying and his brothers are conspiring to keep his inheritance, so she sends him on a bus ride to find a lawyer.

 
July 25 8:00
Harvey
Henry Koster, 1950 104m

Elwood P. Dowd (Jimmy Stewart) is an affable, absent-minded man who lives with his sister Vita but much prefers the company of Harvey, a six foot three and three quarter inch tall white rabbit—a pooka to be exact. Though only Elwood can see Harvey, he insists on introducing the bunny to most everyone he meets. Vita eventually has had enough with her brother's shenanigans and has him (and Harvey) committed. The doctors are taken by Elwood's charm. In the end the doctors, and even Vita, must admit to seeing Harvey. 35mm

 
July 26 7:00, 9:30
The Killer
John Woo, 1990 110m

Starring veteran bad-ass Chow Yun-Fat (The Corruptor), The Killer tells the story of an assassin who becomes obsessed with a woman he accidentally blinded, and the intricate relationship he develops with the cop who pursues him. A predecessor of both Face/Off and Heat, this is the cross-over action movie of the 1990s. Director John Woo (Face/Off), presents us with a ferocious ballet as he gives us beautifully choreographed shots of violence in Hong Kong. 35mm

 
July 27 7:00, 9:30
Zero Effect
Jake Kasdan, 1998 115m

In this hilarious detective's tale, a paranoid genius, Daryl Zero (Bill Pullman) is a recluse who tortures his sharp assistant (Ben Stiller) with his god-awful music and lifestyle. His new case, however, leads him to an unexpectedly worthy opponent: love. As the self-declared “world's greatest detective”, Pullman delivers a convincingly neurotic performance as the ever-quirky Zero. Kasdan's refreshing and ingenious script makes this film a must-see; Pullman and Stiller are at their absolute best. 35mm

 
July 31 8:00
Arsenic and Old Lace
Frank Capra, 1944 118m

Mortimer Bruster (Cary Grant) puts his honeymoon on hold when he becomes entangled in his aunts' unique hobby: poisoning lonely old men and burying them in the basement. Bruster tries to save his aunts by placing the blame on an insane cousin who thinks he's Teddy Roosevelt and digs graves in the basement for yellow fever victims working on the Panama Canal. Arsenic is a slapstick comedy that brings dealing with crazy family members to a new level. 35mm

 
August 1 8:00
My Fair Lady
George Cukor, 1964 170m

This is the classic remake of George Bernard Shaw's Pygmalion (later remade into She's All That).  A bet between two men leads Henry Higgins (Rex Harrison), an arrogant linguistic professor, to remake a woman into someone more attractive, leading in the end to true love. Yet, instead of the dorky, pretty/ugly girl in She's All That, the woman is Eliza Doolittle (Audrey Hepburn), a commoner. My Fair Lady is one of the greatest musicals of all time. 35mm

 
August 2 7:00, 9:30
The Maltese Falcon
John Huston, 1941 101m

Adapted from Dashiell Hammett's novel, The Maltese Falcon is one of the foundational works of film noir.  The film follows detective Sam Spade (Humphrey Bogart) on his search for a stolen, jewel-encrusted falcon. Spade encounters others who are also in search of the valuable bird. In addition to ushering in the film noir genre and iconizing Bogart, The Maltese Falcon debuts a number of formidable film talents, including director John Huston and Sydney Greenstreet, who was nominated as best supporting actor for his role. 35mm

 
August 3 7:00, 9:45
L.A. Confidential
Curtis Hanson, 1997 136m

This glamorous adaptation of James Ellroy's monster novel was nominated for nine Academy Awards, including Best Picture and Best Director. An all-star cast, including Russell Crowe, Guy Pearce, Kevin Spacey, and Kim Basinger, turns in a perfect performance in this intense but polished film that oozes with style. L.A. Confidential is a tale of police corruption and the Hollywood that supports it; but, of course, this is all off the record, on the QT, and very hush-hush! 35mm

 
August 7 8:00
I'll Never Cry
Kwon-Taek Im, 1974 95m

Im (Chunhyang) comes from a past of hardship shaped by the Korean War. Nevertheless, he has made a name for himself internationally for creating complex commentary on Korean life without ever becoming inaccessible to Korean audiences. I'll Never Cry tells the story of a girl sent to reform school and her relationship with the crippled and sickly son of her parole officer, who houses her despite her violent temper. Shot and presented in black and white cinemascope.

 
August 8 8:00
Diabolique
Henri-Georges Clouzot, 1955 110m

This mature drama follows the actions of the wife and mistress of a sadistic school headmaster as they plot his murder. They decide to drown him and dump his body in a pool, but when the body disappears and sightings of the headmaster are reported, the two women find themselves entangled in an impossible mystery far beyond simple murder. Clouzot's Diabolique is a masterful study of pure suspense that is guaranteed to have your heart pounding.

 
August 9 7:00, 9:45
The Sting
George Roy Hill, 1973 129m

A beautiful combination of costume design, art direction and ragtime score by Marvin Hamlisch expertly evokes a Depression-era Chicago. When Luthor, the mentor of Johnny Hooker (Robert Redford), gets killed trying to con a mob-man, Hooker enlists the assistance of Luthor's old friend Henry Gondorff (Paul Newman) to avenge his death. The two work their "sting" on the mob-man by playing upon his greed and love of high-stakes poker. 35mm

 
August 10 7:00, 9:15
Muppets Take Manhattan
Frank Oz, 1984 93m

Frank Oz and Jim Henson team up in the last feature length film starring the original Kermit, Fozzie, Gonzo, and Ms. Piggy. After graduating from college, Henson's merry band of Muppets attempt to take Broadway by storm with their variety show, but end up proving that even Muppets have trouble making it in the real world. Ralph Burns (Cabaret) composed the music for the film, which intertwines the big stage production numbers with sincere melodies. 35mm

 
August 14 8:00
Nayak
Satyajit Ray, 1966 120m

Written and directed by Satyajit Ray, the man who introduced the West to Indian film, Nayak concerns a popular film star who boards a train to get an award. He ends up in a conversation with an earnest young woman that becomes an interview for her newspaper. Throughout their conversation, other sub-plots emerge related to the interaction between the star and the reporter. Ray invests the film with the humanism that became his trademark.

 
August 15 8:00
The Good, the Bad and the Ugly
Sergio Leone, 1967 161m

This spaghetti western made Clint Eastwood an icon as “The Man With No Name”, a gunslinger with a desert-warped sense of honor. Crossing the American West during the Civil War, he and a partner encounter a group of dead and dying Confederate soldiers, one of whom discloses the location of buried gold. Ennio Morricone's legendary musical theme underscores Leone's classic widescreen portrayal of human brutality playing itself out amidst stunning beauty. 35mm

 
August 16 7:00, 9:30
Notorious
Alfred Hitchcock, 1946 101m

U.S. government agent Devlin (Cary Grant) asks Alicia Huberman (Ingrid Bergman), the beautiful daughter of a dead German spy, to marry one of her father's old Nazi buddies and spy on his activities. She falls in love with Devlin and agrees to his plan. But while Alicia uncovers German secrets, her own secret activities are uncovered by her new husband and mother-in-law. Both sides must pretend to know nothing while they try to escape their predicament.

 
August 17 7:00, 9:15
Orlando
Sally Potter, 1993 92m

Inspired by Virginia Woolf's novel, this is the story of a man named Orlando who has eternal youth, and lives through four centuries of experiences, even becoming a woman. The film challenges convention as the gifted Tilda Swinton (The Deep End) plays Orlando, while Queen Elizabeth is played by Quentin Crisp. Under the graceful and elegant direction of Sally Potter, Orlando is a beautiful film that examines the very meaning of human existence. 35mm

 
August 21 8:00
Breakfast at Tiffany's
Blake Edwards, 1961 115m

Holly Golightly (Audrey Hepburn) is a rising New York socialite famous for her wild yet sophisticated flat parties. Fred (George Peppard) meets Holly, leading to a series of attempts to get her to stop courting Brazilian coffee magnates and French bachelor dukes.  Based on the Truman Capote novel, the classic romance story even stars the not-very-convincingly Japanese, but still comedic Mickey Rooney. With a name like “Holly Golightly”, it's got to be good. 35mm

 
August 22 8:00
Faust
Jan Svankmajer, 1994 97m

A man emerges from the depths of a Prague subway to be given a map. The map leads him to a courtyard and a strange theater containing a copy of Goethe's Faust.  Reading it, he descends into a world that is part Goethe and part Svankmajer: a kinetic mix of live action, puppets, stop-motion, clay-mation, and other unique Czech surrealist tricks. The journey is a mix of observational participation, with references to Marlow's Doctor Faustus and Kafka. 35mm

 
August 23 7:00, 9:45
Do the Right Thing
Spike Lee, 1989 120m

This films centers around Sal, a delivery boy, who is only one of Do the Right Thing's eccentric characters. Through careful character development, the film demonstrates race issues without being biased towards either side. The film depicts a day in Brooklyn that ends in tragedy. With the film's rich dialogue and inventive cinematography, this is Spike Lee's masterpiece that explores how racism leads inevitably to tragedy, and that it must somehow end. 35mm

 
August 24 7:00, 9:30
The Last Seduction
John Dahl, 1994 110m

Linda Fiorentino stars as Bridget Gregory, a strong and diabolical woman who plots to get rid of her husband (Bill Pullman), after taking off with $700,000 of illegal drug funds. In a small town, she meets Mike (Peter Berg) in a bar, works her charm, and before long he unwittingly becomes an accessory to murder. Directed by the smart and cynical John Dahl (Red Rock West), this film makes no effort to escape through morality and admires evil instead of condemning it.

 
August 28 8:00
Klute
Alan J. Pakula, 1971 114m

When a businessman disappears, the FBI has only one clue to follow: a New York City call girl name Bree (Jane Fonda). John Klute (Donald Sutherland) comes to the city in order to find Bree. Although their relationship is hesitant at first, they soon grow closer when someone begins to stalk Bree. Under the direction of Alan Pakula (All the President's Men), Sutherland and Fonda make a strong pair in this dark and lustful world, with Jane Fonda winning an Academy Award for her performance. 35mm

 
August 29 8:00
Gaslight
George Cukor, 1944 114m

In this Victorian thriller, George Cukor (Philadelphia Story) earns his reputation as “the woman's director” for coaching Ingrid Bergman through an Oscar winning performance. As the disturbed Paula (Bergman) moves into her murdered aunt's mansion with her new husband, things just don't remain where she puts them. Are her belongings conspiring to undermine her sanity? And why does the gaslight dim every evening while her husband is away? 35mm

 
August 30 7:00, 9:00
Following
Christopher Nolan, 1999 70m

Following focuses on a young man who is obsessed with following strangers to learn about them. When he meets Cobb, his intriguing foil, he descends into a dark underworld that ends in a triple-cross. Time shifts, Nolan's beloved trick, give this elaborate plot a depth of mystery and uncertainty, adding to the film's noir feel. This is Christopher Nolan's directorial debut, before he went on to direct the popular Memento and now Insomnia. He does not disappoint. 35mm

 
August 31 7:00, 9:30
The Conversation
Francis Ford Coppola, 1974 113m

A conversation in the opening credits proves to be a crucial moment, around which this terrifying psychological thriller is based. Harry Caul (Gene Hackman) is a surveillance expert for a big company boss.  A former incident on the job -- a triple murder -- causes him to worry that it will happen again this time. The plot breeds worries of invasion of privacy and oppressive governments -- even murder. Coppola directs the movie with minimal actual violence. 35mm
