Blank Tasks


October 6            7:00
La Belle Noiseuse
Jacques Rivette, 1991 240m

French New Wave director Jacques Rivette collides with Balzac's 19th Century novella,  The Unknown Masterpiece. While the ruffled artist Frenhofer (Michel Piccoli) huffs and puffs through the motions of masterpiece-romanticism, tasks are placed on equal planes and pinning the canvas becomes as important as painting it. The screen remains in-process with constant inscriptions on the soundtrack: cicadas stir the leaves, a fountain pen scratches a sketchbook to release its ink, a bell rings in the distance; the film reminds us of the air within the belfry before the hammer hits.        35mm


October 13            7:00
Seventh Continent
Michael Haneke, 1989 111m

A jarring, hollowed-out work from the director of the provocative Code Unknown (2000). Based on various news reports and events, this feature casts a cold eye on the everyday ups and downs of a middle class family in Austria.  Waiting for their favorite television programs to reach syndication, browsing the magazine racks at the supermarket, the household yearns for a continent undiscovered by travel agencies.  Until then the family car needs to be washed again, the alarm clock obeyed, and the bills paid.  The methodical pace creates a numbing effect that builds to a disturbing and shocking conclusion.    35mm


October 20            7:00
Goalie's Anxiety at the Penalty Kick
Wim Wenders, 1971   101m

Joseph Bloch, an off-duty football goalkeeper, has no match to win. He watches a movie he's seen before, drinks too much at the bar, and falls down in the rain. He makes a sandwich and commits a murder before finding fascination in the mechanics of a jukebox. It's the last line of defense: "It's very difficult to stop watching the action, to keep your eyes on the goalkeeper as he patiently waits for the play to come his way; your eyes keep going back, following the forwards."  Based on the novel by Peter Handke, who co-wrote Wings of Desire with Wenders.


October 27            7:00
Walkabout
Nicolas Roeg, 1971     95m

A family picnic goes awry, forcing a teenage girl and her younger brother to fend for themselves in the vast Australian outback. Orphans in school uniforms, the two become reluctant bricoleurs, making a stick function as a faucet and tying knots in a blanket until it tightens as a knapsack. Roeg's photographic zeal and dynamic editing make the desert landscape a complex terrain and the camera recalls flashes of flesh with only occasional moments of preciousness. To complain of Roeg's heavy hand is to miss the insect-scurrying edge upon which the film rests, even if condominiums and pools are never far away.   35mm 


November 3            7:00
The Gleaners and I
Agnes Varda, 2000      82m

Gleaners pick at already-harvested fields for the odd potato or turnip; they insist on finding a use for material that is considered unfit by others. Varda's unburdened camera tactics (digital video images transferred to 35mm) glide from the forgotten corners of the French countryside to the off-hours at the green markets in Paris. An added bonus is the work of artist Sarah Sze, whose pieces appear to continue growing long after they have been installed. Sze appreciates a spot where a tiny box of crayon-colored tacks once hung from a rack in a pharmacy that is on the verge of becoming a franchise.                    35mm


November 10            7:00
Belfast, Maine
Frederick Wiseman, 1999       248m

America's great documentary filmmaker, Frederick Wiseman, depicts life in a New England port city, with an emphasis on the town's economic dependence on the seafood industry. He captures the tasks of lobstermen, tug-boat operators, shop-owners, choir directors, judges, policemen, teachers, nurses, and ministers.  The characters proceed in earnest, and yet a sense of waiting hovers over many of the portraits. The viewer goes from the motion of machines at a fish packing plant to a morning before a bakery opens. Traffic signals sway over empty streets, and there is something Hopper-like in the early morning light.


November 17            7:00
Celine and Julie Go Boating
Jacques Rivette, 1974  193m

From French New Wave giant Jacques Rivette (Va Savoir, L'Amour Fou) comes this magical and dream-like film, whose title can also be translated as Celine and Julie Go Crazy. Celine (Juliet Bertot, who co-wrote the script) and Julie (Dominique Labourier) meet two ghostly women while in a haunted house. The progression of time threatens their intimacy and the girls decide to return to their childhood in order to alter the past. Jean-Marie Sénia's exquisite soundtrack works in everyday sounds, such as heels clicking against the pavement and the purr of taxi motors.                 35mm


November 24            7:00
Van Gogh
Maurice Pialat, 1991    155m

Pialat's film desentimentalizes the madness that has often overwhelmed Van Gogh's biography.  Accumulated details create a sense of immediacy, allowing commonplace objects and actions to resonate at the expense of the narrative.  History is heard through heavy coins sliding across a counter or the reflex knock on a kneecap that resounds only within the silence of a 19th Century doctor's office.  Van Gogh (Jacques Dutronc) exists in the gaps between working and living, spending time staring into space, while Pialat reverses steps and creates staccato movements to construct a cinema of walking.         35mm 


December 1             7:00
Jeanne Dielman
Chantal Akerman, 1975           198m

Focusing on a housewife who turns tricks before setting silverware for the family dinner, director Chantal Akerman and cinematographer Babette Mangolte use long, wide-angle shots that give everyday rituals an unnerving and defamiliarizing impact. Everyday sounds, objects, and actions reverberate beyond their function, creating blocks of noise, stasis, and movement. Coffee beans are ground to abstraction; the percolator is an hourglass where drips mark the minutes. Later, a meatloaf will weigh down an already claustrophobic kitchen. Jeanne peers beyond her tabletop as though its edge were a precipice.          35mm

Post-War German Cinema


September 30  7:00
Lissy
Konrad Wolf, 1957     89m

This film focuses on Lissy Schroder, a young department store clerk caught up in the political turmoil of Germany in the early 1930s. Her husband joins the Nazi Party after a conflict with his Jewish boss, and his sentiments are not uncommon in their working-class Berlin surroundings. Berlin of the 1930s-- Berlin of Brown Shirts, Berthold Brecht and bold caberet-- comes alive in detail and mood as a background for personal relationships in Lissy. Konrad Wolf, one of the founders and most highly acclaimed directors of DEFA Studios, had escaped Germany in 1933 and returned in 1954.


October 7        7:00
Divided Heaven
Konrad Wolf, 1964     109m

Produced during the brief cultural thaw of the early 1960s, this film was strongly influenced by Alain Resnais' Hiroshima mon amour in form and in its exploration of the dangerous quest for a female identity against the backdrop of momentous historical events. In this case, it is the building of the Berlin Wall that divided East from West Berlin.  With its bold cinematic fragmentation, Divided Heaven anticipated the bans imposed on numerous films in 1965 for displaying too many of the objectionable qualities of decadent Western "new wave" movements. Divided Heaven disappeared deep into the archives to be rediscovered only after Germany's reunification.


October 14      7:00
A Call Girl Named Rosemarie
Rolf Thiele, 1959         100m

In 1957, a Frankfurt call girl named Rosemarie Nitribitt was murdered. It soon was revealed that her services were highly regarded in some of West Germany's most prominent industrial circles and the Nitribitt case was never solved. When this film was chosen to represent West Germany at the Venice Film Festival, the German Foreign Ministry vigorously protested - and it's little wonder why. The film marks the highpoint in the career of Nadja Tiller, who received offers to work with, among others, Antonioni, Fellini and Visconti - all of which she refused.


October 21      7:00
Trace of Stones
Frank Beyer, 1966       139m

Banned by the East German government for its portrayal of working conditions in their country, Trace of Stones provides an excellent visual document of the mid-1960s GDR. Hannes Ballas (Manfred Krug) is the undisputed leader at the Schkonka construction site, using unconventional methods to compensate for the errors and incompetence of the party-appointed administration. When a new party official takes over at the construction site, the status quo seems jeopardized. Eventually, the new man comes around to seeing things Ballas' way, but by this time Ballas finds himself in trouble for an extra-marital love affair with a new engineer.                35mm


October 28      7:00
Yesterday Girl
Alexander Kluge, 1966            88m

A key film that helped launch New German Cinema, Kluge's stunning first feature follows Anita Giestern, a Jewish refugee from East Germany who commits petty theft as she finds her way through an inhospitable Federal Republic hoping for a better life. 
The Middle of the Road is a Very Dead End
A. Kluge & E. Reitz, 1974      90m

Four plot lines are interconnected only through a montage that suggests that the events portrayed are happening simultaneously and in the same place. The lines begin to comment on one another, raising suspicions about causal relationships.


November 4     7:00
Why Does Herr R. Run Amok?
Rainer Werner Fassbinder, 1970         88m

In this film, Fassbinder's interest in violence, falseness, and oppression finds expression in the life of Herr R., a technical draftsman. Herr R. lives a perfectly normal life: he gives his wife all the money her social aspirations demand, he visits his parents, he talks to his son's teachers and friends. Yet he commits, without warning, a most horrendous act of violence.
Chinese Roulette
Rainer Werner Fassbinder, 1976         86m

An unfaithful couple, their disabled daughter and her dumb governess, a housekeeper and her son all come together at a country house.  Fassbinder creates a psychological ballet that ultimately unfolds through the game of Chinese roulette.


November 11   7:00
The Naked Man on the Athletic Field
Konrad Wolf, 1974     99m

Solo Sunny
Konrad Wolf, 1979     102m

Sunny is a pop singer in a mediocre band who longs to be happy and recognized as someone special. After she gets kicked out of the band she starts over in the underground scene in East Berlin. This film addressed the longings of East German Youth while capturing the GDR version of '70s style in its gritty-glitzy visuals and its soundtrack. Its success promised a youthful renewal in DEFA, yet two discoveries among its youth talent, Renate Krößner and Eberhard Geick, pursued their careers in the West.35mm


November 18   7:00
Ticket of No Return
Ulrike Ottinger, 1979  108m

Social parody and exaggeration dominate the work of the avant-guard feminist Ulrike Ottinger.                      35mm

REDUPERS
Helke Sander, 1977     98m

"Most women work harder than men.  They do things they want to do when they have already spent sixty hours working on something else," said Sander, who both directed and played the leading role in her first fiction film. The film reveals three days in the life of a woman and a photographer, and three days in  West Berlin in March 1977. It brilliantly questions the construction of the identities of both the women and the city.    35mm


November 25   7:00
Ludwig, Requiem for a Virgin King
Hans-Jürgen Syberberg, 1972 134m

The first in his trilogy (followed by Karl May and Hitler, a Film from Germany), Syberberg presents this wildly eclectic meditation on Ludwig II of Bavaria, who was a homosexual, drug addict, Francophile, and pacifist who built story-book castles and supported Richard Wagner. Germanic cultural and political allusions combine into a visually arresting and intellectually challenging film structured in 28 chapters. It references recent German history (Hitler) with German culture (particularly music and especially Wagner) to probe the seeming incompatibility of beauty and cruelty.


December 2     7:00
Germany, Pale Mother
Helma Sanders-Brahms, 1979 149m

Sanders-Brahms, a descendant of Johannes Brahms, used her childhood and her mother's life at the time of the Second World War as the source for Germany, Pale Mother. Titled after Bertolt Brecht's poem, this controversial and moving account begins with the courtship of a young couple on the eve of the German invasion of Poland, through the war, where mother and daughter build a bond in the father's absence, and into the post-war period. The film takes an extremely personal perspective as it struggles not only with the historical issues of the war on the German side, but also the relationship between mother and daughter.


Rockumentary


October 1        7:00
Don't Look Back
D.A. Pennebaker, 1967           96m

"Bob Dylan's Yankee caravan moves through a dreary, unswinging Britain." And while Dylan plays sphinx to an endless stream of reporters and works on his metamorphosis from down-to-earth folkie to withdrawn rocker, Pennebaker's hands-off style lets us watch Dylan's mask slip as he struggles between affection and disaffection. This is Bob Dylan "at his most despotic, a gaunt silhouette with stovepipe legs and a fabulous corona of unkempt hair," says Janet Maslin. As enjoyable for Dylan's harrowing attacks on the press as it is for his music.   35mm

           
October 8        7:00
Monterey Pop
D.A. Pennebaker, 1968           78m

On the heels of Don't Look Back,  Pennebaker and Albert Maysles (Gimme Shelter) turned his lens on the 1967 Monterey Pop Festival, the precursor to 1969's Woodstock.  As usual, Pennebaker allows the events to speak for themselves, Here, that includes the live performances of Simon & Garfunkel, Janis Joplin, Otis Redding, The Who, Jefferson Airplane, The Mamas and the Papas, Ravi Shankar, and the appearance of the new Jimi Hendrix Experience with the amazing guitar sound that would ignite the music world in the years to come (Hendrix, for his part, ignited his guitar at the end of his set).                          35mm


October 15      7:00
Gimme Shelter
A. Maysles & D. Maysles, 1970         91m

Originally designed to follow the course of the Rolling Stones' 1969 tour for their album, Let it Bleed, Gimme Shelter ultimately captured a picture of mayhem and tragedy. The film documents the Stones' infamous concert at Altamount Speedway, which resulted in four deaths, including the murder of Meredith Hunter by a group of Hell's Angels hired to act as security for the concert. Members of the Stones helped edit the film, and one senses the ominous mood that permeated production of the film. Including amazing live performances by Tina Turner, Jerry Garcia, and the Stones, the film presents the era in its glorious, grisly, decadent whole.           35mm


October 22      7:00
Last Waltz
Martin Scorsese, 1978 117m

It is The Band's last concert, and it's going to be a wild ride. It's Thanksgiving Day, 1976, and The Band has been going for more than fifteen years. Martin Scorsese directed the documentary, and appears himself along with an impressive array of The Band's friends and other performers, including Ringo Starr, Bob Dylan, Neil Diamond, Joni Mitchell, Eric Clapton, Muddy Waters, Emmylou Harris, Robbie Robertson, and a full brass band.  Scorsese brings a particular flair to the direction of the film, while Pennebaker and Maysles observe all at a distance, letting the music speak. Scorsese enters the very heart and spirit of the music.                    35mm


October 29      7:00
The Filth and the Fury
Julien Temple, 2000    108m

The Sex Pistols put out just one album and then self destructed within two years. Yet, for its short lifetime the band provided a soundtrack for a British population plagued by unemployment and social upheaval. They also cursed at the press, spat on their audience, got banned from TV, and used enough drugs to make any punk rocker proud. The Filth and the Fury is the third documentary of the rise and lightning quick demise of the band, this time, from the viewpoints of the band members themselves (rather than their self-promoting manager, Malcolm McLaren.) Rare performance footage and wickedly funny interviews make this film a rollicking good joy ride.        35mm


November 5     7:00
The Decline of Western Civilization
Penelope Spheeris, 1981         100m  
While many know Director Penelope Spheeris from Wayne's World, it was her earlier work and knowledge of music that landed her that job. Her directorial debut, The Decline of Western Civilization, descends to the depths of the L.A. punk scene of the early 1980s. Spheeris follows Circle Jerks, Fear's Finale, X, The Germs, Alice Bag Band, and Catholic Discipline from their night club venues to their homes. The Germs' Darby Crash reveals his drug abuse in candid terms, while lamenting his ostracism from venues where he had previously performed. He would later overdose before the film's release.      35mm


November 12   7:00
Stop Making Sense
Jonathan Demme, 1984           88m

Filmed in just three nights at the Holly-wood Pantages Theater, Stop Making Sense revolutionized the rockumentary by focusing entirely on the stage performances of its band, the Talking Heads, without interruption for interviews with band members or shots of crazed fans. Long shots (some lasting almost a minute) allow the audience to enjoy the full visual impact of performance art presentations of "Psycho Killer," "Once in a Lifetime," and "Heaven." The Heads' infectious musical style, which includes elements of pop, reggae, funk, and be-bop, is beautifully combined with an as eclectic visual style: think jumpsuits, aerobics, and '80s shoulder pads. 35mm


November 19   7:00
This is Spinal Tap
Rob Reiner, 1984        82m

This rockumentary spoof from screenwriters Christopher Guest and Michael McKean (who would team up again to write Waiting for Guffman and Best in Show) chronicles Spinal Tap, a British band destined for, well... obscurity. Spinal Tap is on their latest U.S. tour, which appears to be a downward spiral. When asked about the loss of fans, manager Ian Faith dismisses the comment - the fans are simply becoming more "selective". Their tour is an overly-theatrical, expensive disaster; the band's members quarrel like prima donnas; and the album is a flop. A perfect satire/rockumentary of "VH1: Behind the Music" melodrama.                  35mm


November 26   7:00
Hype!
Doug Pray, 1996         87m

The northwest rock explosion started out small in the '90s, with bands performing their new grunge sound sampling from whatever they could, and recording themselves. Grunge, popularized by Nirvana and sensationalized by Kurt Cobain's suicide, became an untapped goldmine to studio executives. Seattle quickly established itself as a national music industry locus surrounded by plenty of marketing hype. Director Doug Pray seems bemused by the business, but there's no joking about the music. Includes live performances and interviews with Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, Mudhoney, and many others.   35mm


December 3     7:00
Meeting People is Easy
Grant Gee, 1999          99m

Documenting Radiohead on its 1997-98 tour, following the release of their epic, Grammy-winning album, OK Computer, Meeting People is Easy is a melancholy look at the perversities of celebrity. Singer Thom York is overwhelmed by the fans that scream his name.  Band members bemoan the materialism and alienation that celebrity has foisted upon them and look far more comfortable when they are in the studio recording in peace. Switching between video and film, Director Grant Gee (who directed the group's "No Surprises" video) creates a surreal atmosphere that echoes what it must be like amid the swirl of flashing light bulbs and screaming fans.        35mm

Contemporary Mexican Cinema


October 2        7:00
Like Water for Chocolate
Alfonso Arau, 1991     123m

Two appetites, sex and food, combine to drive this narrative about Tita (Lumi Cavazos) who, beholden to family tradition, must care for her overbearing mother despite her love for Pedro (Mario Leonardi). Tita unleashes her passions in the culinary feats she performs for her family, as she eventually overthrows the oppressive traditions that bind her. The film is rendered in lush sepia tones that convey a romantic view of Mexican ranch life juxtaposed against a backtdrop of the Mexican Revolution. A major international hit upon its release, Like Water for Chocolate cultivated a global audience for the Mexican cinema that followed.                     35mm


October 9        7:00
Danzón
María Novaro, 1991     120m

In the danzón, "the man seduces, the woman shines." Or does she? Julia (María Rojo), who works by day as a long-distance telephone operator, has been dancing the slow, ballroom step with Carmelo for six years, but knows nothing about the man. When he inexplicably disappears, she discovers that he meant more to her than she realized, and heads to Veracruz in search of him. There, she befriends a drag queen and others. Searching for a man she barely knows, Julia finds herself instead. Novaro, one of the leading figures of the New Mexican Cinema, inverts generic conventions and, in the process, transforms what it is that constitutes mexicanidad.          35mm


October 16      7:00
Cabeza de Vaca
Nicolás Echevarría, 1991         111m

This historical drama focuses on Alvar Nunez Cabeza de Vaca, one of five survivors of an ill-fated 600-man Spanish expedition off of Mexico's eastern coast in 1528. The film chronicles Cabeza de Vaca's enslavement to a powerful shaman of the Iguase tribe, his education in magic, and eventual eight year trek across Mexico to the Pacific coast. Director and ethnologist Echeverría takes great pains to carefully render the culture of an American Indian river tribe before the introduction of European influences. Native speech is not translated and long expanses of the movie are silent as Cabeza de Vaca attempts to comprehend the culture he finds himself in.    35mm


October 23      7:00
Angel of Fire
Dana Rotberg, 1993    95m

Dana Rotberg's Angel of Fire follows a young fire-eating trapeze artist who carries on a love affair with her ailing clown father.  When officials discover she is pregnant, she is fired and joins an evangelical puppet troop. On par with Joderowsky's Santa Sangre and Buñuel's Los Olvidados, Angel of Fire intertwines a surrealist vision with religious typologies to deliver a stunning critique of life at the social and spatial margins of contemporary Mexico City. Rotberg fashions images that are as beautiful as they are haunting, refusing an exotic view of Mexico by revealing the unreal effects modernization has had on those it alienates.                           35mm


October 30      7:00
Deep Crimson
Arturo Ripstein, 1996  109m

In a career that has spanned over four decades, Arturo Ripstein remains Mexico's foremost filmmaker. Deep Crimson, written by longtime collaborator Paz Alicia Garciadiego, continues his investigation of the underside of human nature. Based loosely on the Lonely Hearts Murders of elderly widows in the Midwest and New England in the 1940s, the film centers around Coral (Regina Orozco), who falls passionately in love with Nicolas (Daniel Giménez Cacho), leaving her children for him. Posing as siblings, the two enter a life of swindling and murdering desperate women in the borderlands. Wickedly funny, melodramatic, and truly horrifying.     35mm


November 6     7:00
Who the Hell is Juliette?
Carlos Marcovich, 1997          91m

While filming a music video, Carlos Marcovich met two fatherless women: Yuliet, a 16-year jintera (prostitute) in a poor barrio of Havana, and Fabiola, a twenty-three year old Mexican model. The film examines the ambivalent longing and anger each has for her father. Alternately effervescent, knowing, adolescent, and profound, Yuliet captivates our attention in a series of short episodes that impressionistically hang together like a diaphanous tapestry. Co-written with Carlos Cuarón, the film mixes documentary with fiction, with a poignant cameo by Selma Hayek.       35mm                                     


November 13   7:00
Guilt Free
Marcel Sisniega, 1996 100m

>Two brothers react differently to the death of their father. Esteban, the older brother, flees to Europe for a year, returning to Mexico to write a novel. His brother Juan, an aspiring poet, focuses himself on his studies and falls in love with Aurora, one of his classmates. Both live with their mother, who assuages her grief by retreating further into religion, even taking in seminarians as boarders. Built upon a parable of Cain and Abel, this film is a meditation on the ways in which love animates frustration, sorrow, and even hatred. With an award-winning score by Zbigniew Paleta.      35mm


November 20   7:00
Amores Perros
Alejandro González Iñárritu, 2001       154m

A car chase through the streets of Mexico City results in a crash upon which this epic triptych is hung. Octavio (Gael García Bernal) is a teenager who gets embroiled in illegal dog fighting to win his brother's wife. Daniel is a midde-age married man who falls in love with a model. El Chivo (Emilio Echevarría) is a former revolutionary who is now a disheveled tramp and part-time assassin. The three character's interlocking stories provide a meditation on love's varied effects, proving that "love is for the dogs." They also provide a survey of the phases of life and the sharp class divisions that intensified during the seventy-one years of single party rule of Mexico.        35mm


November 27   7:00
Chronicle of a Breakfast
Benjamin Cann, 1999  120m

Chronicle of a Breakfast is a wildly stylized look at love, sex, and family. A husband comes home late one night to make up with his wife after eight years.. As the new day dawns, the children realize their father has come home — and they're not especially happy about it. His daughter runs away, his older son dreams of beating him, and his younger son dreams of dragons. Taking place in one morning, the film uses flashbacks to illustrate the joys and sorrows that created this dysfunctional family. Along the way, we encounter a motley assortment of characters, including a bitter transsexual in a search of her penis and a flying fat woman.                               35mm


December 4     7:00
Missing Young Woman
Lourdes Portillo, 2001 74m

Across the border from El Paso, Texas in Ciudad Juárez, young women work shifts in excess of twelve hours in maquiladoras, the foreign- (mostly U.S.-) owned assembly plants along the Mexico-U.S. border. Missing Young Woman tells the story of the over 200 kidnapped, raped and murdered young women of Juárez. While authorities have known about the murders since 1993, young women continue to "disappear" and there is little hope that perpetrators will be brought to justice. Portillo movingly  and poetically documents the fear, horror and anger of the families of the murdered young women, revealing the underbelly of our global economy.                              35mm

In The News


October 3                    7:00
The Front Page
Lewis Milestone          , 1931  101m

Before there was His Girl Friday, there was The Front Page, adapted from the popular Ben Hecht and Charles MacArthur play. The film presenented audiences for the first time with the hard-drinking newsmen, their cynical disregard for all things secular, and their rapid-fire dialogue.  Adolphe Menjou, Pat O'Brien, Edward Everett Horton, and Mae Clarke star as smart-mouthed and wisecracking journalists waiting in the Chicago Criminal Court Building's pressroom for a murderer to be hanged when the convict escapes.


October 10                  7:00
Five-Star Final
Mervyn LeRoy, 1931  89m

Edward G. Robinson, Boris Karloff, and H.B. Warner star in this social commentary damning the muckraking press and their shameless and immoral tactics. When the owner of the New York Gazette wants a boost in circulation, he stumbles upon the idea of reviving a sensational murder from 20 years in the past. His headlines about the scandal result in two suicides, which also make the front page and ruin several happy lives. Five-Star Final was one of the first sound films to expose and challenge the scandal sheet.


October 17      7:00
Foreign Correspondent
Alfred Hitchcock, 1940           120m

A fresh, young reporter (Joel McCrea) drops into the intrigue, kidnapping, and murder of European politics on the eve of World War II. Eventually, he links up with another reporter, ffolliot (George Sanders), and the daughter of a prominent peace activist. Hitchcock fans will especially love the master's use of William Cameron Menzies' architectural set pieces, including the Amsterdam square and an intercontinental airliner. The film is based on Vincent Sheean's book Personal History.


October 24      7:00
The Philadelphia Story
George Cukor, 1940    112m

Cary Grant, Katherine Hepburn and Jimmy Stewart give magnificent performances in Cukor's expert entry into the newspaper genre. Tracy Lord (Hepburn), recently divorced from C. K. Dexter Haven (Grant), now plans to marry the more responsible George Kittredge (John Howard). Spy magazine sends Haven to cover the story with reporter Mike O'Connor (Stewart). Lord presents a steadfastly certain demeanor, but it soon becomes apparent that her heart is not as clear as her head.


October 31      7:00
Deadline - U.S.A.
Richard Brooks, 1952 87m

Humphrey Bogart stars as crusading editor Ed Hutcheson of the independent New York newspaper The Day.  When the socially conscious paper is sold to a strictly commercial venture, Hutcheson welcomes the chance for respite and reunion with his wife (Kim Hunter). But when one of his reporters suffers a servere beating at the hands of mob boss Tomas Rienzi, Hutchinson must race to expose the mob boss and his connection to a murder before the paper's production ceases.


November 7     7:00
Park Row
Sam Fuller, 1952             83m

In the 1880s, Park Row was the street at the heart of New York newspaper publishing, complete with statues of Ben Franklin and Horace Greeley. The ideals of Franklin and Greeley are the ideals of Phineas Mitchell, who finds himself unable to work for bigshot Charity Hackett. With the backing of like-minded individuals, Mitchell starts a new paper which springs to life in time to support the construction of a base for the Statue of Liberty. However, Hackett will not give up without a fight - literally.


November 14   7:00
While the City Sleeps
Fritz Lang, 1956          100m

The sensational serial killings of young women has taken all the headlines in New York. At the New York Sentinel, a high-level power struggle leaves Walter Kyne (Vincent Price) in general control of the paper. Kyne promises the top executive spot to the Sentinel employee who can find the serial killer. At first, the hard-drinking but noble veteran reporter Edward Mobley (Dana Andrews) refuses to participate in the stunt, but finally relents and dangles his own fiancée before the madman as bait.


November 21   7:00
The Day the Earth Caught Fire
Val Guest, 1961           98m

In London, Fleet Street reporters think something is amiss with the global weather conditions. It's hot and foggy in England, flooding in France, and fires rage in Australia. There is an unscheduled eclipse of the sun. Stenning (Edward Judd) and Maguire (Leo McKern), the scientific editors for the Daily Express, realize the Earth is falling closer to the sun. Director Guest uses the convention of the newspaper film to lend to credibility to this end of the world scenario.


November 28   7:00
All the President's Men
Alan J. Pakula, 1976    138m
