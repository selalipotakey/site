CUT IT OUT: Collage and Montage in the Avant-Garde

 
March 31

Jordan/Klahr

Two films by masters of cut-out animation! Larry Jordan's The Rime of the Ancient Mariner (1977) puts the great French illustrator Gustave Doré's 1875 engravings of the Samuel Taylor Coleridge poem into motion, with narration performed by Orson Welles. Lewis Klahr, who cites Larry Jordan and Harry Smith as important influences, has been deemed the reigning king of "cut and paste" by critic J. Hoberman. Klahr's epic The Pharaoh's Belt (1993) uses magazine ads from the 1950s and 60s to create worlds from which characters must, as Tom Gunning writes, "negotiate a labor of extrication from the morass of Betty Crocker chocolate icing." runs 85m 

 
April 7  
7:00
Sonbert/Dorsky

Carriage Trade (1971), the longest film in Warren Sonbert's oeuvre, is a heavily edited silent barrage of imagery in which no shot lasts much more than ten seconds. The 300+ shots, taken from Sonbert's travels through over a dozen countries, depict famous tourist sites and the mundane activities that surround them. Sonbert's Friendly Witness (1989), his first sound film in 20 years, continues his "polyvalent montage" style, but with an added layer: rock music. Nathaniel Dorsky uses a slower-paced montage in his film Hours for Jerome (1980-82), which Sonbert has written "is simply the most beautifully photographed film that I've ever seen."  runs 128m 

 
April 14 
7:00
Stan Brakhage

Stan Brakhage has been a powerful force in avant-garde filmmaking for half a century. Since 1952, he has made almost 300 films with running times that vary from under three minutes to over three hours. Tonight's program offers a chance to see some of the films that earned Brakhage his comparison to Sergei Eisenstein, the great theorist of montage, including The Cat's Cradle (1959) and Anticipation of the Night (1958), which is considered his breakthrough film. Of The Animals of Eden and After (1970), Brakhage has written that the film "is too mysterious, to me, for me to be able to write anything about it except that it seems to be the best film I've ever made."   runs 83m 

 
April 21 
7:00
Just a Movie

Beginning with Bruce Conner's A Movie, the found-footage masterpiece from the late 1950s, tonight's program samples the sampling that has become a standard in recent avant-garde film. Pornographic materials are refashioned, interrogated, and meditated upon in an amazing variety of ways in Peggy Ahwesh's The Color of Love (1994) and Scott Stark's Noema (1998), among many others. In Naomi Uman's Removed (1999), female bodies are bleached out to leave men having intercourse with white voids. Also included is Martin Arnold's Pièce Touchée (1989), which expands an 18-second sequence into 15 minutes of stuttering and then exhilirating motion. runs 122m 

 
April 28 
7:00
Breer/O'Neill

Robert Breer's Recreation (1956) and Rubber Cement (1976) are just two examples of the playful films made by this famous animator. Collisions between line drawings, live-action footage, found material, and music and natural sound draw us into Breer's energetic and clever constructions. Pat O'Neill, who runs a special effects company and has been described as a technical wizard for his work with the optical printer, worked for ten years on Water and Power (1989) and for 17 years collecting ideas, images, and sounds for Trouble in the Image (1996). O'Neill describes Trouble as "made up of dozens of performances dislodged from other contexts."   runs 104m

7:00
Abigail Child

Is This What You Were Born For? is a series of seven films, made between 1981 and 1989, by poet and filmmaker Abigail Child. The films are dense, filled with imagery from film noir, early cinema, pornography, home movies, and other sources. Child's approach to the cutting of image and sound creates a virtuoso mosaic. The films are powerful investigations of the portrayal of women and the construction of identity and sexuality in pop culture. Surface Noise (2000) takes on a sonata form, again highlighting Child's skill in constructing and manipulating complex sound montage and continuing her investigation of issues of labor and class. runs 74m

Larry Jordan

Once Upon a Time (1974) provides an example of the short cut-out animations that are the bulk of Jordan's output. But like The Rime of the Ancient Mariner, Sophie's Place (1986) extends Jordan's animation prowess to a longer format. Hand-painted and cut-out images create a magical world inspired by Sophia, goddess of wisdom, and revolve around the mosque of Saint Sophia in Constantinople. The film is a rapid montage of magical objects that travel across enchanted backgrounds, all transforming themselves mysteriously, all lushly colored and meticulously composed. The landscapes Jordan creates are complex and visually stunning.  runs 102m

Reports

In Report (1963-67), an unforgettable meditation on the assassination of JFK, Conner uses news footage and images from American mass culture to critique both the media and our implicit participation in the spectacle of violence. Peter Kubelka's Unsere Afrikareise (1961-66), Sonbert's Noblesse Oblige (1981), Craig Baldwin's Tribulation 99 (1991), and Conner's Television Assassination (1963-95) all similarly recombine images and sounds to question and often subvert their original contexts. runs 113m

SPRINGTIME IN PARIS

Co-sponsored by the France Chicago Center and the College in anticipation of the fall 2003 opening of the University of Chicago Center in Paris. Funding also from the Cultural Services of the French Embassy, Chicago.

 
March 25 7:00
Children of Paradise
Marcel Carné, 1945 190m

Written by poet Jacques Prevert, this film shows the theatrical Paris of epic romance, where words are sung, gestures are danced, and love's throws are as light and intricate as lace. Its main characters - the pantomime artist Baptiste Debureau, the romantic actor Frederick Lemaitre, and the criminal Lacenaire - all fall in love with the beautiful actress/Aphrodite Garance (Arletty). In a plot that twists like pre-Haussman streets, the three lovers find and lose each other in the dense crowds parading along the Boulevard du Crime. Flawlessly executed and with a peerless cast, this is one of the great French films.  35mm 

 
April 1  7:00
Hôtel du Nord
Marcel Carné, 1938 95m 

Doomed lovers Reneé (Annabella) and Pierre take a room at the Hôtel du Nord in order to carry out a suicide pact. When the plot fails, Pierre escapes, while Reneé becomes a maid at the hotel and joins its family of characters, including Raymonde (Arletty), a vivacious and resilient prostitute, and Edmond, her pimp. Based on a book by Eugène Dabit, whose parents owned the real Hôtel du Nord, the film won the Prix populiste for its depiction of the people of the northeast Paris neighborhood in which it is set. The film's success led to the preservation of the hotel, which now has a restaurant named "Atmosphère! Atmosphère" - one of the film's key phrases.

L'Atalante
Jean Vigo, 1933 89m

When Juliette (Dita Parlo) marries Jules, a Seine barge captain, the villagers proclaim that "she always had to do things differently." Despite the couple's obvious affection for one another and the romance of river travel (beautifully rendered in black & white), Juliette and Jules still have to learn to live with each other, just like everyone else. They contend with the added challenges of minimal space, an omnipresent crew, and a profusion of cats. Away from home for the first time and lured by radio broadcasts from the capital, Juliette dreams of visiting Paris, but when she and Jules are prevented from seeing the city, will fulfilling her desire destroy her marriage? 35mm

Zazie in the Underground
Louis Malle, 1960  89m

Foul-mouthed brat Zazie is sent to stay with her uncle, a female impersonator, in Paris while her mother has a rendezvous with her latest lover. Zazie terrorizes and escapes her uncle and sets out to explore the city by metro, encountering possible child molesters, Marxist revolutionaries, and a food fight along the way. Basing the film on a novel by Raymond Queneau, known for his linguistic trickery, director Louis Malle (My Dinner with Andre) described this film as "trying to find some equivalent to what Queneau was doing with literature." Malle draws on slapstick, the Marx brothers, and cartoons to create an arresting visual style and a hilarious satire of French society.

Rendezvous in Paris
Eric Rohmer, 1995 94m

Broken appointments make broken hearts in this assemblage of three tales of young romance and modern times in Paris. In the first story, Esther finds out the real reasons that Horace is endlessly "busy" for dinner. In the second, a man and woman discuss their quasi-affair while wandering through Paris parks. In the third, a painter has a date with a new muse, but follows his eyes to another woman. On se donne des rendez-vous, on y va ou pas, et on tombe sur qui, sur quoi? (Make a date, but do you go or no, and what if you run into somebody else on the way?) The Paris of this film is spontaneous and reserved, avant-garde and old world, trivial and philosophical. 35mm

Breathless
Jean-Luc Godard, 1961 90m

The most famous film of the French New Wave, Breathless exploded onto the international scene with stylistic innovations and attitude to spare. Wanted criminal Michel (Jean-Paul Belmondo) tries to convince a young American (Jean Seberg) to flee with him to Italy. 35mm 

 
May 20 7:00
Hate
Mathieu Kassovitz, 1995 96m

In this melo-docu-drama, three friends seek retribution when their best friend suffers violence in police custody. An Arab, a Jew, and a black African, they represent groups that tend to remain outside the péripherique, the highway that encloses Paris. Residents of les banlieues, where much of the city's subsidized housing is located, the friends form kinship out of their alienation from the city and against regular sweeps of their neighborhood by police. Director Mathieu Kassovitz draws on Spike Lee's 1989 Do the Right Thing, but Kassovitz's portrayal of rage among les banlieusards focuses more closely on the details of their mounting rage than on its stunning explosion.  35mm 

 
May 27 7:00
What Time Is It There?
Tsai Ming-Liang, 2001 116m

Hsiao-kang, a young street vendor who sells watches in Taipei, becomes obsessed with a beautiful customer who says she is moving to Paris, and he sells her his own wristwatch. Fixated on the idea of her living "in another time," he compulsively sets all the clocks in Taipei back seven hours to Paris time, even risking his life to reach clocks at the tops of city buildings. Through a series of strange and cinematic coincidences, the two cities gradually seem to converge. Director Tsai Ming-Liang (The River) adds to his darkly humorous blend of compulsion, alienation, and superstition by using cinema and time zones to highlight the modern distortions of time and space.  35mm

Paris, Texas
Wim Wenders, 1984 147m

After wandering in the desert for four years with amnesia, Travis (Harry Dean Stanton), presumed dead by family and friends, resurfaces to confront the life he lost. His brother Walt (Dean Stockwell) tries to remind Travis of his previous life, when he walked out on his wife and son. While attempting to rebuild his relationship with his seven-year-old son, Hunter, Travis also tries to locate his disillusioned wife, now working in a peep show. Winner of a number of awards, including top honors at Cannes in 1984, the film uses sparse, insightful dialogue and boasts an excellent soundtrack that suits the sun-bleached landscapes and melancholic undertones.  35mm

Brewster McCloud
Robert Altman, 1970 105m

Bud Cort, the actor famous for his role in Harold and Maude (and for his subsequent vanishing) stars as young Brewster McCloud in this early Robert Altman fable. Brewster is a bookish, modern Icarus, constructing enormous wings in his home, the fallout shelter of the Houston Astrodome, while getting fired from a long string of odd jobs. He dreams of one day flying freely in the Astrodome. People who cross him tend to die in freakish bird-related incidents, but he doesn't mean any harm. . . This is Altman at his sprawling, epic best, framing idiosyncratic human drama in the middle of the paved and deserted landscape of urban Texas.

Reality Bites tells the story of four Generation-X friends experiencing the trials and tribulations of post-collegiate depression. Vickie Miner (Janeane Garofalo) works at the Gap, Lelaina Pierce (Winona Ryder) is a production assistant to a crotchety old man with a bad talk show, Troy Dyer (Ethan Hawke) floats in and out of meaningless jobs while contemplating existential bullshit, and Sammy Gray (Steve Zahn) comes to terms with his sexuality. Ben Stiller, playing the corporate sell-out, mixes things up by competing with Troy for Lelaina's love. Reality Bites holds its own as a tribute to slacker life in young America.   35mm

Waco: Rules of Engagement
William Gazecki, 1997 136m

www.waco93.com and www.pissantprod.com

A controversial documentary of the highly publicized standoff in the spring of 1993, this film opposes the FBI's claims that David Koresh and his followers, the Branch Davidians, were a fringe cult that had themselves started the fire which consumed their compound. The film uses footage from the siege itself, the ensuing congressional hearings, and interviews with technical, psychological, and religious experts. The filmmakers strongly suggest that the group was in fact legitimate under the first Amendment, falling victim to the ineptitude of an ATF (Alcohol Tobacco and Firearms) raid and the subsequent prolonged standoff with FBI agents. 35mm

Slacker
Richard Linklater, 1991 97m

This film wanders aimlessly through the streets of Austin, Texas, following one pensive twenty-something after another as they try to articulate their personal beliefs to anyone willing to listen. Characters include a UFO buff who adamantly insists that the U.S. has been on the moon since the 1950s and a woman who expertly hawks a glass slide of Madonna's pap smear. Remarkable for its fluidity, Slacker covers a lot of ground around the sprawling neighborhoods of Austin and maintains a bleak backdrop that is never quite forgotten, even behind all the zaniness of the young, idealistic deadbeats and social outcasts that keep the film interesting.  35mm

Urban Cowboy
James Bridges, 1980 132m

In James Bridges's irresistibly clichéd love story of a working man and his ex-girlfriend, Bud Davis (John Travolta) is a strapping young farm lad who moves to the city to work in an oil refinery. Who wouldn't want to catch John Travolta decked out in a cowboy hat and chaps "lookin' for love in all the wrong places?"

Hands on a Hard Body
S. R. Bindler, 1998 94m

Hands on a Hard Body documents the 24 entrants of an annual contest sponsored by the local Nissan dealership in Longview, Texas. The winner, who is the last one standing with his or her hand on the car, goes home with a brand-new, fully-loaded pickup truck. The sleep-deprived competition is so fierce that the last three survivors must be tested for drugs. Among the competitors are a number of quirky characters, including a woman who communes with Jesus by the truck. After 78 hours of torture, who will emerge triumphant? Don't miss this off-beat microcosm of American life, revealing just how far people will go for a new set of wheels.   35mm

ARCHITECTURE IN CINEMA 

 
March 28 7:00
L'Inhumaine
Marcel L'Herbier, 1924  135m

This rarely screened showcase of French artists of the 1920s features the set designs of architects Rober Mallet-Stevens and Alberto Calvacanti. Cubist exteriors converge with extravagant Deco interiors and geometric lab machines to create an early masterpiece of architectural and cinematic collaboration. The sci-fi/thriller/love story plot follows the glamorous Claire Lescot (American singer Georgette Leblanc) and two rivals for her affections, leading us on a melodramatic path from feigned suicide to the revival of the dead. 

 
April 4  7:00
Playtime
Jacques Tati, 1967 152m

Architecture has a starring role in Jacques Tati's comic and stylistic masterpiece, filmed on a massive set outside Paris using thousands of square meters of concrete, wood, and glass. The film focuses on modern architecture and the influences of technology, turning nondescript buildings and offices into confusing and intimidating spaces. Foregoing any conventional narrative, Tati presents a series of events revolving around M. Hulot and his encounter with a group of American tourists. A perceptual and conceptual tour de force. 35mm 

 
April 11 7:00
Just Imagine
David Butler, 1930  102m

Probably the first science fiction musical ever, Just Imagine offers a cheery response to Fritz Lang's dreary view of the future in Metropolis. New York City is presented fifty years in the future with an entirely changed skyline and urban organization, nine levels of traffic, and private airplanes. The protagonist, Single O, hit with a golf ball in 1930, wakes up from fifty years of suspended animation and witnesses the love story of J-21 and LN-18. Stephen Goosson's sets reference Hugh Ferriss's 1929 visionary drawings of skyscrapers. 35mm 

 
April 18 7:00
Things To Come
William Menzies, 1936 100m

William Menzies and writer H. G. Wells respond to Metropolis by surveying a century in the life of an English "Everytown," beginning in 1936, and presenting the antithesis of Lang's skyscraper city. Designers incorporate the minimalist horizontality of the modern movement into the vision of 2036. This landmark of "prophetic science fiction" chronicles the decades of post-war suffering and the eventual triumph of technology and reason, until a rocket launch to the moon spawns a debate about the use of technology.

Stephen Goosson's Academy Award-winning set blends formal elements from the European avant-garde (Mallet-Stevens and Le Corbusier) with cantilevered roofs that emphasize horizontality (think Robie House). 35mm

My Uncle
