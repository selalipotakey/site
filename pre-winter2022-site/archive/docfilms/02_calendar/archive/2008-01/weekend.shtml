Friday, January 11 - 6:45, 9:00, 11:15, 4:00 matinee
Eastern Promises
David Cronenberg, 2007 - 100 min.
As he did in A History of Violence, Cronenberg focuses on the lives of the criminal and marginalized rather than on his usual body horrors. When nurse Naomi Watts comes into possession of the diary of a dead Russian prostitute, her journey toward deciphering it leads her straight into the hands of the Russian mob, which wants its secrets silenced at all costs. Featuring fine performances from Watts, Viggo Mortensen, and Vincent Cassel, and one of the more memorable cinematic fights in recent years, Eastern Promises enthralls and shocks. 35mm.
Saturday, January 12 - 6:30, 9:00, 11:30, 1:00 matinee
Across the Universe
Julie Taymor, 2007 - 131 min.
The most phantasmagorical pop pastiche this side of Moulin Rouge!, Across the Universe reimagines the '60s as a fab repository of love, music, and revolutionary chic. There a romance between the Lucy and Jude, the travails of a closeted lesbian named Prudence, and a Magical Mystery Tour with Bono! Only Julie Taymor, who tamed The Lion King for Broadway, could possess the audacity to stage musical numbers like "She's So Heavy," in which a group of despondent G.I.s drag the Statue of Liberty through the jungles of 'Nam. 35mm.
Friday, January 18 - 7:00, 10:00, 1:00 matinee
Lust, Caution
Ang Lee, 2007 - 158 min.
Lust, Caution pits Wong Kar Wai-favorite Tony Leung and newcomer Tang Wei together and against one another in this World War II-era spy thriller set in occupied Shanghai. Young university student Wong Chia Chi (Wei) meets with some success in her campus' acting troupe, but soon falls in with a group of students plotting the elaborate assassination of Mr. Yee (Leung), a top collaborator with the Japanese. Loosely based on an Eileen Chang novella, the film is reminiscent of Hitchcock's Notorious, until Lee directs it toward an uncharacteristic height in acrobatic explicitness. 35mm.
Saturday, January 19 - 7:00, 9:00, 11:00, 4:00 matinee
Once
John Carney, 2007 - 85 min.
This surprise indie hit, which has met with critical and popular acclaim, so affected director Steven Spielberg that he proclaimed: "A little movie called Once gave me enough inspiration to last the rest of the year." Glen Hansard, a member of Irish band The Frames, plays an Irish busker who meets and falls for a young Czech immigrant (Czech singer/songwriter Marketa Irglova). The two musicians turn their budding relationship into an album, rehearsing and recording in the hopes of landing a record contract in London. This modern-day musical delights with effortless charm. 35mm.
Friday, January 25 - 7:00, 9:00, 11:00, 4:00 matinee
King of Kong: A Fistful of Quarters
Seth Gordon, 2007 - 79 min.
Hailed as one of the best documentaries of the decade, The King of Kong finds an underdog story where not even Hollywood could find one: the world of competitive video games. Billy Mitchell built a hot sauce empire based on his video game celebrity. No one could even approach his record at Donkey Kong until Steve Wiebe, an unknown, unemployed Seattle resident, shattered it. What follows is a high drama of lies and deception more Shakespearean than arcadean. 35mm.
Saturday, January 26 - 7:00, 10:00, 1:00 matinee
The Assassination of Jesse James by the Coward Robert Ford
Andrew Dominik, 2007 - 160 min.
An avant-garde western shot in the Canadian wilderness by an Australian with Malick-ian aspirations, The Assassination of Jesse James by the Coward Robert Ford is a wide-eyed meditation on the intersections of crime, folkways, and celebrity worship in 19th-century America. It's also a better gay western than Brokeback Mountain, if only for the scene in which a nude Jesse James (Brad Pitt) turns his back to acolyte Robert Ford (Casey Affleck) and implores him to do his worst. 35mm.
Friday, February 1 - 6:45, 9:00, 11:15, 1:00 matinee
Dan in Real Life
Peter Hedges, 2007 - 98 min.
Steve Carell is Dan Burns, a widower with three daughters who writes an advice-for-the-lovelorn column. His own love life is empty until he has a chance encounter with Marie (Juliette Binoche), a woman he meets while in Rhode Island for the annual family reunion. Unfortunately for Dan, Marie just happens to be dating his younger brother (Dane Cook). Sweet, realistic, often humorous, and aided by a surprisingly tender lead performance from Carell, Dan in Real Life is a classic date film, or just the perfect tonic to get you out of those winter doldrums. 35mm.
Saturday, February 2 - 7:00, 10:00, 3:00 matinee
American Gangster
Ridley Scott, 2007 - 158 min.
During the 1970s, Frank Lucas (Denzel Washington) owned Harlem. He cleared a million bucks a day on the high-quality dope Blue Magic, which he managed to sell at half the price of his competitors by importing his shit direct from Indochina in the coffins of fallen soldiers. He trusted only his brothers from North Carolina and forced his low-level employees to work in the nude so they couldn't steal anything. He met his match (according to Scott, not the historical record) in Richie Roberts (Russell Crowe), a straight-arrow cop from Jersey who finally brought him down. Sparks fly. 35mm.

Friday, February 15 - 6:15, 9:00, 11:45, 3:00 matinee
Into the Wild
Sean Penn, 2007 - 140 min.
Based on the bestselling book by Jon Krakauer, Into the Wild recreates the story of Christopher McCandless, an Emory grad who in 1992 gave away his possessions and trekked into the Alaskan wilderness. Along the way, McCandless (sensitively portrayed by Emile Hirsch) encounters a variety of people who are all touched by his warmth and humanity. Sean Penn adapted and directed Into the Wild, focusing on the decision that McCandless made and the society that drove him to it, while glossing over some of the nastier aspects of his personality. 35mm.
Saturday, February 16 - 7:00, 9:00, 11:00, 1:00 matinee
My Kid Could Paint That
Amir Bar-Lev, 2007 - 82 min.
"My kid could paint that!" - a common hillybilly rebuke to the egghead's adulation over a work of abstract 'art.' But also a genuine expression of parental pride when Laura and Mark Olmstead noticed that their four-year-old Marla was turning out Pollock-esque fantasias in daddy's hobby corner. The Olmsteads present Marla's work at a local gallery and before long canvases sell for $25,000. Then 60 Minutes takes an interest. And then Amir Bar-Lev sees the whole bloody affair as a readymade tapestry of cultural tensions, art world absurdity, and documentary ethics. 35mm.
Friday, February 22 - 6:30, 9:00, 11:30, 1:00 matinee
Jimmy Carter: Man from Plains
Jonathan Demme, 2007 - 125 min.
Demme's film is not an exploration of or a commentary on Jimmy Carter's presidency; rather, it follows the ex-President on his book tour for Palestine: Peace Not Apartheid. Carter travels from interview to interview promoting his book and fighting charges of anti-Semitism. The film also captures the 83-year-old's everyday interactions with make-up artists, TV crews, his friends and neighbors from Plains, GA, and his wife, with whom he still reads the Bible daily. The result is a portrait of an elder statesman who surpasses his predecessors in quiet dignity. 35mm.
Saturday, February 23 - 7:00, 9:00, 11:00, 4:00 matinee
Bee Movie
Steve Hickner & Simon J. Smith, 2007 - 90 min.
Jerry Seinfeld's screenwriting debut, a computer-animated comedy of interspecies romance and litigation, chronicles the efforts of Barry Bee Benson (voiced by Seinfeld) and his human love interest to right the injustices wrought by honey-stealing humans upon hardworking honeybees. Though criticized as entomologically inaccurate, the film's Mr. Smith Goes To Washington meets Ratatouille storyline ultimately carries the heartwarming and biologically accurate message that humans and bees need each other to survive. 35mm.
Friday, February 29 - 6:15, 9:00, 11:45, 1:00 matinee
La vie en rose
Oliver Dahan, 2007 - 140 min.
Edith Piaf, the most singular crooner of the twentieth century, famously sang, 'Non, je ne regrette rien.' But her life was dotted with many events that a less stoic woman would have readily regretted. This biopic trudges through it all - the brothels, the blindness, the morphine, the brutes. Sounds harsh, but Marion Cotillard's note-perfect impersonation of Piaf is something to see. She'll probably have earned an Oscar for this performance by the time you read this - and, indeed, she makes misery such a joy to behold. 35mm.
Saturday, March 1 - 7:00, 9:00, 11:00, 4:00 matinee
The Darjeeling Limited
Wes Anderson, 2007 - 91 min.
Wes Anderson's new comedy finds him as whimsical and heartfelt as ever in its tale of three estranged siblings' journey across India in an attempt to get over their father's death. While the film is full of pathos, comic misadventures remain prominent: the three endure an illegal cobra purchase, romantic intrigue with a pretty train attendant, and innumerable skirmishes in which familial resentments boil over in Anderson's singularly farcical manner. Featuring cameos by Angelica Huston and Bill Murray, Darjeeling Limited is a fine addition to the Anderson canon. 35mm.
Friday, March 7 - 6:30, 9:00, 11:30, 4:00 matinee
Control
Anton Corbijin, 2007 - 121 min.
In the late 70s, English band Joy Division burst onto the music scene. The group, led by enigmatic lead singer Ian Curtis, became an enormous success. However, on the eve of their American tour and the height of their fame, Curtis committed suicide, abandoning his mistress, his wife and child, and his music career. The mystique of Ian Curtis surrounds the music of Joy Division to this day. Director Anton Corbijn attempts to strip Curtis' story of the hagiography and myth-building of other rock biopics, filling his debut film with stunning black-and-white photography. 35mm.
Saturday, March 8 - 6:30, 9:00, 11:30, 1:00 matinee
Michael Clayton
Tony Gilroy, 2007 - 119 min.
If the tagline of Gilroy's movie - "The Truth Can Be Adjusted" - has a ring of "The Conspiracy is Global" and "Everything is Connected," that's because Michael Clayton is a worthy political thriller in the mold of The Constant Gardener and Syriana. This one is a little quieter, but no less scalding: George Clooney plays a 'fixer' for a powerful law firm in the midst of untangling an Enron-like corporate scandal. Clayton glimpses the madness at the core of corporate culture while repairing damage done by an unhinged partner and sparring with a brash counsel (Tilda Swinton). 35mm.
Friday, March 14 - 6:30, 9:00, 11:30, 4:00 matinee
Beowulf
Robert Zemeckis, 2007 - 113 min.
Hwaet! No one expected Zemeckis and veteran cyberpunk screenwriter Neil Gaiman to adapt the Anglo-Saxon epic to film any more faithfully than featuring its most memorable battles as action sequences dripping with special effects. But in this, it does a good job. Grendel looks the part of a cadaverous draugr, the dragon appears lifted from a D&D sourcebook, and Angelina Jolie brings suppleness to a creature once defined solely by a vague sense of gruesomeness. And who won't quiver to see the most disarming scene in hallowed literature done justice? 35mm.
Saturday, March 15 - 6:30, 9:00, 11:30, 1:00 matinee
Enchanted
Kevin Lima, 2007 - 107 min.
It's packed with parodies of Disney fairytale classics, but Enchanted tells a tale as sweet as any of the ones it spoofs. When the animated Princess Giselle falls through a magic portal, she ends up in the alien world of live-action Manhattan (in the form of Junebug's Amy Adams). Navigating New York traffic, a dragon, and the cynicism of the divorce lawyer she befriends (Patrick Dempsy) with charming idealism, a CGI chipmunk, and many songs (from the folks who wrote Wicked), Giselle learns that true love can be as powerful in the real world as in any fairytale kingdom. 35mm.
