RACE MOVIES: BLACK CINEMA BEFORE 1950

"Race Movies" are black-cast films made before 1950 for segregated African-American audiences. They range in genre from melodramas, thrillers, and musicals to comedies and Westerns. These independent productions provided black viewers with images of African-American experience that were conspicuously absent from Hollywood films, including black romance, urban migration, social upheaval, racial violence, alcoholism, and color prejudice within the black community. "Race Movies" by maverick African-American directors such as Oscar Micheaux and Spencer Williams laid the groundwork for later black filmmaking, from the commercial success of "blaxploitation" to the stylistic references and social commentary of Charles Burnett, Julie Dash, and Spike Lee.

Novocaine
                                   
Within Our Gates   7:00
Oscar Micheaux, 1920    99m
During the summer of 1919, there were race riots in more than twenty U.S. cities. In Chicago, the local censor board initially censored this film because its "8,000 Feet of Sensational Realism" included references to interracial rape, concubinage, and lynching. Telling the story of an educated black woman with a shocking past (Evelyn Preer), Within Our Gates is Micheaux's second film and the earliest extant feature film by an African-American director. This screening is sponsored by The Center for the Study of Race, Politics and Culture. With live musical accompaniment.  Restored 35mm

January 20            2:00
Mulholland Drive
           
Body and Soul             7:00
Oscar Micheaux, 1924    104m
Starring Paul Robeson as an ex-convict-turned-preacher and his twin, a mild-mannered inventor, this film is artistically and politically radical. Robeson and Micheaux used the form of double roles to expose and explore dualities within the community when its coherence and religious leadership were seen as crucial to its political advancement. Not a conventional realist work, Body and Soul violates the Hollywood codes of storytelling. Using flashbacks and dream sequences, Micheaux's editing seeks simultaneity over continuity. With live jazz accompaniment by the Damian Espinosa Trio.


January 27            2:00
Waking Life
           
Ten Nights in the Bar Room            7:00
Roy Calnek, 1926    65m
Based on a popular temperance play performed in black communities, Ten Nights features two of the leading black stage actors of the day: Lawrence Chenault as a bar room proprietor and Charles Gilpin as a drunkard. The production company, the Colored Players Film Corporation, was founded in 1926 by white producer David Starkman, who made popular melodramas about black families and communities. Ten Nights enjoyed the longest continuous run of any race film in the silent era: four weeks at New York City's Grant Theatre. With live musical accompaniment by Funkadesi.         35mm

From Hell
           
Scar of Shame             7:00
Frank Perugini, 1927            92m
Elite pianist Alvin (Harry Henderson) marries lower-class Louise (Lucia Lynn Moses), but their social differences cannot be reconciled. A melodrama of class conflict, this film is one of four produced by the Colored Players Film Corporation for the "race movie" audience. Writer David Starkman hired a white director and white technicians but worked with an all-black cast. The film is notable for its high production values and its rare depiction of color prejudice and class tensions within the black community. With live jazz accompaniment by Fred Anderson. Oscar Micheaux, 1932    44m
Micheaux frequently recycled story material, and this film is a remake of his 1925 silent The House Behind the Cedars, an adaptation of the novel by noted black author Charles Chestnutt. Micheaux reinterprets Chestnutt's story: here the light-skinned heroine, Rena, subverts the "tragic mulatta" trap by enthusiastically choosing to claim her black identity and a dark-skinned black entrepreneur boyfriend. One of the last films made by the great Spencer Williams, Juke Joint is a fun-filled all-black-cast comedy about a pair of hitch-hiking con artists named Bad News Johnson and July Jones. The Devil's Church
Hans Mierendorff, 1919    56m

FILM AND MEMORY


January 8            7:00
Hiroshima, Mon Amour
Alain Resnais, 1959    90m
Marking a coming of age for European modernist cinema, Alain Resnais's first feature is a complex portrait of a French film actress and a Japanese architect who have a brief affair in post-war Hiroshima. Juxtaposing the contemporary landscape with the woman's recollections of her affair with a Nazi soldier, the film lies somewhere between memory and fantasy. With voice-over dialogue by the lead characters, sudden breaks from the story action to documentary footage of the 1945 atomic bombing of Hiroshima, and generous use of flashbacks, Resnais plays the love story against our collective memory of the city's nuclear destruction. A masterpiece of lyrical storytelling.


January 15            7:00
Total Recall
Paul Verhoeven, 1990    113m
Inspired by Philip K. Dick's short story "We Can Remember It For You Wholesale," this action/sci-fi film tells the story of Douglas Quaid Hauser, a construction worker who dreams of living on Mars, but whose wife would never let him go. Enter Rekall, the memory implantation firm that promises him an "ego trip" - a virtual memory holiday. He chooses to have the memory of a secret agent programmed into his mind, but the implant seems to go wrong; he remembers really being a Mars Intelligence Officer and finds that his wife and friends are trying to kill him. Is his newly realized memory actually "reality," and was his "normal" life merely an illusion?             35mm

12 Monkeys
Terry Gilliam, 1995    129m
Bruce Willis plays a man sent back in time to stop mankind's imminent demise and clear up a haunting childhood memory. Sent too far back, he is hospitalized and branded insane.               35mm
La Jetée
Chris Marker, 1962    28m
This short film from renowned film essayist Chris Marker tells the story on which 12 Monkeys was based. La Jetée is almost without precedent, featuring complicated depictions of memory and a compelling take on destiny. Made up almost completely of still photographs united by voice-over narration and Marker's fantastic sense of rhythmic editing, this is a science-fiction masterpiece.


January 29            7:00
Solaris
Andrei Tarkovsky, 1972    167m
"Can a man truly reconcile with his irretrievable past, or is he inexorably bound to the guilt and regret of his spiritual longing?" Andrei Tarkovsky's adaptation of Stanislav Lem's seminal (meta)science-fiction novel is a hypnotic, dream-like epic on love, faith, and reconciliation. Psychologist Kris Kelvin (Donatas Banionis) is assigned to visit a mysterious oceanic planet called Solaris, where two scientists inform him of the Ocean's supernatural ability to materialize their thoughts. Kelvin is forced to confront the now-tangible memories of his deceased wife. In long, meditative images, the film's chronological disunity foregrounds issues of memory and time.       Scope


February 5            7:00
Eve's Bayou
Kasi Lemmons, 1997    109m
Told in flashback, Eve's Bayou is about a well-to-do 10-year-old Creole girl (Jurnee Smollett) growing up in mid-1960s Louisiana who tries to resolve the changing impressions she has of her parents and her upbringing. Writer and director Kasi Lemmons weaves a complicated fabric of memories, hallucinations, dreams, and the supernatural in this impressive directorial debut, while the remarkable cast, led by Samuel L. Jackson and young Smollett in her film acting debut, nearly steals the film. Narrated by a grown-up woman recalling her past, the entire film is a hazily beautiful and haunting childhood memory. An elegant work of magical realism.              35mm


February 12            7:00
Wild Strawberries
Ingmar Bergman, 1957            90m
This is Ingmar Bergman's tour de force about the elderly Professor Borg (film director Victor Sjostrom), who reminisces about his long life's many regrets as he travels with his estranged daughter-in-law (played with irresistible coldness and radiance by Ingrid Thulin) to receive an honorary degree. The professor's memories unfold in a hallucinatory flashback or dream-like style, as he frequently observes the sudden appearance of figures from his past amidst his present settings. Wild Strawberries presents memory as the key component in a voyage of discovery - a passage from estrangement to enlightenment and a new, freed capacity to love and be loved.


February 19            7:00
Jacob's Ladder
Adrian Lyne, 1990    115m
Adrian Lyne's underrated gem tells the Pirandellic horror story of Jacob Singer (Tim Robbins), a postal worker who begins to see gruesome, nightmarish visions on the streets of New York City. Memories of his lost son, his failed marriage, and an attack on his Vietnam combat troop haunt him, and he is forced to confront his twisted, tortured psyche as he feels increasingly threatened with death. As Jacob holds on tighter to what he thinks is reality, his visions become more horrific and more intimately personal. The grainy film stock and Maurice Jarre's sparse score perfectly complement the claustrophobic, oppressive, hazy representation of New York.     35mm


February 26            7:00
Shoah
Claude Lanzmann, 1985    273m
This documentary masterpiece is probably the most compelling and haunting cinematic memory of the Holocaust. More than a simple record of events, it is an oral history that removes the comfort of temporal distance from the horrific events by placing them in the present. Claude Lanzmann pointedly avoids depicting or reenacting scenes of destruction from the 1940s, relying instead on recent interviews with Holocaust survivors, murderers, and bystanders to create a testament to some of the most regretful acts of our history. This is the first half; the remaining 290 minutes will be screened on Saturday, March 2 at 12:00 noon.       35mm


March 5            7:00
Mirage
Edward Dmytryk, 1965            108m
Gregory Peck stars as David Stillwell, a New York accountant who begins to think he is coming out of a two-year stint of amnesia. As he tries to piece together the truth and his memory comes back to him, he finds himself caught up in a conspiracy plot revolving around the death of an executive who plunged from the building he was in the day that he lost his memory. The film makes clever use of flashbacks, a staple of noir and other crime thrillers, and it proves that the amnesiac conspiracy suspense film does not begin with Christopher Nolan's Memento. Walter Matthau delivers a fine performance as Ted Caselle, a sympathetic rookie private investigator.       35mm




KUBRICK


January 9            7:00
The Killer's Kiss
Stanley Kubrick, 1955            67m
Stanley Kubrick's first widely released film. A prize fighter attempts to court a private dancer and protect her from her violent gangster boyfriend. Kubrick wrote, directed, produced, edited, and photographed The Killer's Kiss on the streets of New York on a shoestring budget, filming mostly on location and often illegally. A gritty, stylistic film noir. 35mm
The Killing
Stanley Kubrick, 1956            85m
The Killing is about a "foolproof" race track robbery that entirely falls apart. It works almost like a jigsaw puzzle, filmed with raw noir aesthetics and a documentary-like narrative style. This is the film that put Kubrick on the map.             35mm


January 16            7:00
Paths of Glory
Stanley Kubrick, 1957            86m
At the age of 29, Kubrick transitioned from his early work to the epic scale of this film. Colonel Dax (Kirk Douglas) is a French infantry colonel in World War I placed in charge of a suicide mission against a heavily guarded enemy position in the trenches. After the majority of his unit retreats, the commanding officer demands that three men be arbitrarily tried and executed for cowardice. Dax attempts to save their lives, while raising questions about the validity of the trial itself, the failed mission, and, on some level, war in general. Kubrick's movies are often thought to be timeless in a particular way, and this film may have been the first of those films.               35mm


January 23            7:00
Spartacus
Stanley Kubrick, 1960            198m
In some ways an antidote to the romanticism of biblical epics like Ben-Hur, Spartacus is a sweeping, beautifully filmed studio epic set during the Roman Empire. It replaces the light tones of Ben-Hur with somber browns and reds, and instead of adrenaline-building action features horrifying war scenes sometimes too difficult to watch. Notorious for its controversial content and for the various power conflicts that ensued during production (between Kubrick and the film's star and producer, Kirk Douglas, the studio, and crew members), this film was at least partially responsible for Kubrick's insistence on complete creative control over his later pictures.                           35mm


January 30            7:00
Lolita
Stanley Kubrick, 1962            152m
Kubrick was the first director gutsy enough to adapt Vladimir Nabokov's novel about a sexual relationship between a middle-aged man named Humbert Humbert (James Mason) and a 12-year-old girl. Backed by a major studio in 1962, the result is certainly tamer than the original novel (and Adrian Lyne's recent adaptation) but was quite controversial for its time. The film has its share of sex, but the real energy and scandal derives from innuendo and implication, which Kubrick exploits to full effect thanks to Nabokov's adaptation of his own work - and, in particular, to performances by Shelley Winters as Charlotte and Peter Sellers as Clare Quilty.      35mm


February 6            7:00
Dr. Strangelove
Stanley Kubrick, 1964            93m
Originally intended as serious, this film became adapted as a satire and has since come to be one of the few films to successfully mock the idea of a nuclear holocaust. An insane general (Sterling Hayden) sends U.S. bombers to attack the Soviet Union, who informs the President that such an attack will mean full-scale nuclear war. Only three men (all played by Peter Sellers) may be able to stop the imminent tragedy: the U.S. president, a British captain, and a former Nazi genius named Dr. Strangelove. Famous for dozens of lines, scenes, and sight gags, including the Slim Pickens classic. May be the most innapropriately funny movie ever made.         35mm


February 13            7:00
A Clockwork Orange
Stanley Kubrick, 1971            137m
Alex (Malcolm McDowell) is a young man whose "principle interests are rape, ultraviolence, and Beethoven." The film seems to work within these juxtapositions throughout, from the music to the slang Alex and his droogs speak (a mixture of Shakespearean quips and London gutter slang). When young Alex is caught and put in an experimental rehabilitation program, he discovers a form of punishment far worse than prison. Depending on how you look at it, a twisted sci-fi view of the near future, a sharp satire, or a meditation on the present state of things, if not all three at once. Based on the classic novel by Anthony Burgess, who later rejected the adaptation.   35mm


February 20            7:00
Barry Lyndon
Stanley Kubrick, 1975            184m
After making numerous controversial films, Kubrick decided to craft a meticulous, exquisite period piece. Of course, he did it in his own particular way, insisting on no artificial light for night scenes, a wardrobe of entirely antique costumes, and a production design constructed as if every scene were an oil painting. The result is a deliberately slow and engaging portrait of William Makepeace Thackeray's classic Irish rogue hero (played by Ryan O'Neal), who travels the battlefields and parlors of 18th-century Europe seducing, gambling, and dueling his way into nobility - and eventually letting it get to his head. This was Kubrick's biggest Academy Award winner.


February 27            7:00
Full Metal Jacket
Stanley Kubrick, 1987            116m
In contrast to other Vietnam War films released in the 1980s, Kubrick's view of the war is intentionally awkward and structurally disjointed to emphasize the feeling of war rather than the feeling of a cinematic work. Unlike most other war films, which focus primarily on the war itself, this film is split into two segments, the first of which is one of the most traumatizing training sequences ever filmed. Kubrick cast a former army drill instructor (R. Lee Ermey) as the trainer. Ultimately, the film is cold: the narrator, Joker (Matthew Modine), speaks in a monotone about becoming a trained killer, the music is sterile, and the photography feels grimy and ugly.           35mm


March 6            7:00
Eyes Wide Shut
Stanley Kubrick, 1999            159m
Based on Arthur Schnitzler's Freudian 1926 novel, Kubrick's final film was a massive undertaking that became one of his most debated works. Tom Cruise and Nicole Kidman play a married doctor and art curator whose relationship shows signs of tension. After she confesses to nearly having an affair, his jealously drives him to have his own sexual adventure, leading to a number of bizarre and harrowing encounters. Kubrick's meticulous attention to detail shines. Rife with allusions, self-reference, and comedy, this is certainly a film to be interpreted and misinterpreted for years to come. Kubrick reportedly believed that Eyes Wide Shut was his best film.           35mm




GRAHAM GREENE

Ministry of Fear
Fritz Lang, 1944              84m
Stephen Neale (Ray Milland) leaves his sanitarium only to find himself trapped in a nest of Nazi spies in wartime London. The spy universe and unusual characters evoke Alfred Hitchcock's 39 Steps, while the cinematography smacks of German director Fritz Lang's earlier films (Metropolis, M) and completes the stylish noir look of the film. As a film critic, Ministry of Fear novelist Graham Greene routinely gave good reviews to Lang's films and bad reviews to Hitchcock, whom he successfully blocked from filming Our Man in Havana.                   35mm

Herman Shumlin, 1945            122m
During the Spanish Civil War, Denard, a Republican messenger, arrives in England to buy coal for his superiors. By profession, he is not an agent but a musician, and Charles Boyer's performance as Denard adds an appropriate sad tiredness reflective of the years of conflict he has endured. Inhabiting the twilit world of pre-war England with Denard are a number of characters of uncertain allegiance, including a possible love interest (Lauren Bacall). Franz Waxman wrote the score for this atmospheric adaptation of Greene's novel.      35mm

This Gun For Hire
Frank Tuttle, 1942           80m
Angel of death Philip Raven (Alan Ladd) is a professional hit man with a soft spot for cats and children. When he is double-crossed by Willard Gates, he goes after revenge and comes across showgirl/undercover agent Ellen Graham (Veronica Lake), girlfriend of a police lieutenant who is pursuing Raven. Instead of wasting her on the train, Raven finds out that she is working undercover against Gates and they form an uneasy alliance. Their strange friendship emotes what is otherwise a dark and overtly political thriller.       35mm


January 24            7:00
Brighton Rock
John Boulting, 1947    88m
In this fine example of British noir, teen gang leader Pinkie (Richard Attenborough) runs a small protection racket in the seaside town of Brighton. When he discovers that his latest murder was witnessed by a young waitress, he courts and marries her in order to keep her quiet. A Catholic in his own manner, Pinkie rationalizes his increasingly violent and desperate acts as his gang members begin to question his erratic behavior. Greene adapted his 1938 novel with playwright Terence Rattigan (The Browning Version).      35mm


January 31            7:00
The Fallen Idol
Carol Reed, 1948    95m
Philippe, the son of a London ambassador, couldn't be happier when his parents leave him for the weekend with their butler, Baines (Ralph Richardson). Working-class Baines is a hero to the young lad, having regaled him with tall tales from his past, but as Philippe becomes embroiled in the death of Mrs. Baines and the subsequent accusations against Baines, he finds that much of what he believes is not true. Greene co-wrote the script from his story, and this was the first of three collaborations with his friend Carol Reed.


February 7            7:00
The Third Man
Carol Reed, 1949    93m
In this famous atmospheric noir, American pulp novelist Holly Martins (Joseph Cotton) searches through post-war Vienna for  his old friend Harry Lime (Orson Welles). Upon arriving, Martins finds out from English Major Calloway that Lime was a racketeer and is now dead. Martins does not believe the first claim and disproves the second, in the process stumbling through a penicillin racket, accusations of murder, and a tragic love story. Calloway: "That sounds like a cheap novelette." Martins: "Well, I write cheap novelettes."    35mm


February 14            7:00
The Heart of the Matter
G. More O'Ferrall, 1954    105m
In wartime Sierra Leone, Major Scobie (Trevor Howard) tries to live a good life and be a good Catholic. Having sent his wife back to London with money borrowed from a questionable local, however, Scobie falls for another woman and is blackmailed upon his wife's return, forcing him into questions of love, duty, and faith. Filmed in Sierra Leone, where Greene was stationed during the war, some of the finest British actors of the day (Michael Hordern, Denholm Elliot, and Peter Finch) support Howard's moving performance.          35mm


February 21            7:00
The End of the Affair
Edward Dmytryk, 1955            105m
The embittered protagonist of this story, Maurice Bendrix (Van Johnson), struggles to understand his past relationship with a married woman, Sarah Miles (Deborah Kerr). As he attempts to discover why she left him (suddenly, and just after an air raid no less), he must deal with far-reaching spiritual implications. Lord, I hate You as though You existed. Greene spent his life grappling with issues of faith and religion, but rarely so metaphysically as in this story. Also starring John Mills, Peter Cushing, and Michael Goodliffe.                35mm

The Quiet American

British journalist Thomas Fowler (Michael Redgrave) languishes in Vietnam, smoking opium, neglecting his duties, and living with his girlfriend, Phuong. When American Alden Pyle (Audie Murphy) appears and offers Phuong Western domesticity and political reform, an ideological battle for her heart and mind ensues. Producer and director Joseph L.
